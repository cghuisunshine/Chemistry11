8.1 Hydrocarbons & Isomerism

Key ideas

  • Organic Chemistry: The study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon forms 4 bonds, allowing for complex chains and rings.
  • Hydrocarbons: Contain only Carbon and Hydrogen. Subdivided into Alkanes (single bonds), Alkenes (double bonds), Alkynes (triple bonds), and Aromatics (benzene ring).
  • Isomers: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements (Isomers have different properties).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alkane: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (Saturated).
  • Alkene: CₙH₂ₙ (Unsaturated).
  • Alkyne: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (Unsaturated).
  • Naming (IUPAC): Prefix (branches) + Parent chain + Suffix (family).

Short example(s)

  • Propane: C₃H₈ (Alkane).
  • Butane vs 2-methylpropane: Both are C₄H₁₀, but different structures (Isomers).

8.2 Functional Groups

Key ideas

  • Functional Group: A specific group of atoms that defines the chemical reactivity of an organic molecule.
  • Reactvity: Reactions occur at the functional group, while the carbon chain (R-group) remains mostly stable.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Alcohol: R-OH (Ending: -ol).
  • Carboxylic Acid: R-COOH (Ending: -oic acid).
  • Ester: R-COO-R' (Ending: -oate).
  • Amine: R-NH₂ (Basic).

Short example(s)

  • Ethanol: C₂H₅OH (Alcohol found in wine).
  • Ethanoic Acid: CH₃COOH (Vinegar).
  • Ethyl Ethanoate: CH₃COOC₂H₅ (Fruity ester).

8.3 Organic Reactions

Key ideas

  • Combustion: Burning in Oxygen. Complete combustion produces CO₂ and H₂O.
  • Substitution: Replacing a Hydrogen atom with another atom (e.g., Halogen). Common in Alkanes (needs UV light) and Benzene.
  • Addition: Breaking a double/triple bond to add atoms. Common in Alkenes/Alkynes.
  • Esterification: Acid + Alcohol → Ester + Water. (Dehydration Synthesis).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Esterification: R-COOH + R'-OH → R-COO-R' + H₂O.
  • Addition: C=C + X-Y → C(X)-C(Y).

Short example(s)

  • Bromination of Ethene: C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂ (Orange color disappears quickly).
  • Polymerization: Many small monomers (e.g., ethene) join to form a large polymer (e.g., polyethylene).
