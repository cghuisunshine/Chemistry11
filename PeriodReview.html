Trends in the Periodic Table – Review Answer Key

This key covers atomic radius, ionization energy, and the reactivity of metals in single-replacement reactions and in acid.

Part 1 – Atomic Radius

1. Calcium vs. Scandium and Strontium

a) Would the radius of scandium be bigger or smaller than calcium?

Answer: Smaller.

Across a period, nuclear charge increases and radius decreases, so Sc is smaller than Ca.

b) Would the radius of strontium be bigger or smaller than calcium?

Answer: Bigger.

Down a group there are more energy levels, so atomic radius increases.

2. Lithium vs. Beryllium

Atomic radii: Li = 145 pm, Be = 112 pm. Draw Bohr diagrams and use them to explain.

Key ideas:

  • Both Li and Be have 2 electron shells.
  • Li has 1 valence electron (2,1); Be has 2 valence electrons (2,2).
  • Be has a higher nuclear charge (more protons) pulling the electrons closer.

Conclusion: Be has a smaller radius because its stronger nuclear attraction pulls the same number of shells closer to the nucleus.

3. Fluorine vs. Chlorine

Atomic radii: F = 73 pm, Cl = 99 pm. Draw Bohr diagrams and explain.

Explanation:

  • F and Cl are in the same group (halogens).
  • Cl has one more energy level than F.

Because Cl has an extra shell, its electrons are farther from the nucleus, so its atomic radius is larger than F.

4. Fe, Co, Ni Radii

Atomic radii: Fe = 126 pm, Co = 125 pm, Ni = 124 pm.

a) Electron configurations in core notation and explanation.

  • Fe: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶
  • Co: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁷
  • Ni: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁸

Electrons are being added to the same 3d sublevel. Shielding changes very little, so the effective nuclear charge increases only slightly. This causes only a very small decrease in atomic radius across Fe → Co → Ni.

5. Ni, Cu, Zn Radii

Atomic radii: Ni = 124 pm, Cu = 128 pm, Zn = 138 pm.

a) Electron configurations in core notation and explanation.

  • Ni: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁸
  • Cu: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰
  • Zn: [Ar] 4s² 3d¹⁰

Trend-wise, the radius is expected to keep decreasing across the period. Instead it increases from Ni → Cu → Zn because once the 3d sublevel is full, additional electrons and protons change shielding and electron–electron repulsions. The filled 3d shell shields the 4s electrons more effectively, allowing the outer electrons to be held less tightly and increasing the radius.

Part 1 – Ionization Energy

1. Sodium vs. Magnesium and Potassium

Ionization energy (IE) of Na = 5.12 eV.

a) Would IE of Mg be higher or lower than Na?

Answer: Higher.

Mg has a higher nuclear charge and a smaller radius than Na, so it holds its valence electrons more strongly.

b) Would IE of K be higher or lower than Na?

Answer: Lower.

K is below Na in the same group and has an extra energy level, so its outer electron is farther from the nucleus and easier to remove.

2. Magnesium vs. Aluminum

IE of Mg = 7.61 eV; IE of Al = 5.95 eV.

a) Based on the general trend, should IE of Al be higher or lower than Mg?

Trend prediction: Al should be higher (to the right of Mg).

b) Explain the actual numbers.

In reality, Al has the lower IE. This is because the electron removed from Al is a 3p¹ electron, which is higher in energy and less tightly held than the 3s² electron removed from Mg. Subshell structure (3s vs 3p) causes this exception to the general trend.

3. Nitrogen vs. Oxygen

IE of N = 14.5 eV; IE of O = 13.5 eV.

a) Based on the trend, should the IE of O be higher or lower than N?

Trend prediction: O should be higher (to the right of N).

b) Explain the actual numbers.

The actual IE of O is lower. Nitrogen has a half-filled p sublevel (3 electrons in 3 p orbitals), which is especially stable. Oxygen has one paired electron in a p orbital; electron–electron repulsion makes it easier to remove that electron. As a result, O has a slightly lower ionization energy than N.

Part 2 – Reactivity of Metals Investigation

Key Principle: Single Replacement Reactions

A metal will replace another metal in solution only if it is more reactive (higher in the activity series). If a reaction occurs, the solid metal is more reactive than the metal ion in solution.

Expected Reaction Patterns with Metal Nitrates

Using a standard activity series, the reactions with various metal nitrates can be summarized qualitatively as:

  • More reactive metals (like Mg, Zn, Fe) displace less reactive metal ions from solution.
  • Less reactive metals (like Cu, Pb) do not react when placed in solutions of more reactive metal ions.

Using the Lab to Compare Reactivity

1. How can a single replacement reaction with a metal nitrate show which metal is more reactive?

If metal A placed into a solution of metal B nitrate causes a reaction (metal B solid forms), then metal A is more reactive than metal B. No reaction means metal A is less reactive than metal B.

2. Order of metal reactivity based on the test results (solid metal vs. metal nitrate solutions).

Order from most to least reactive:

Mg > Zn > Fe > Sn > Pb > Cu

Metals in Acid

3. Which of the tested metals are more reactive than hydrogen?

Metals that react with acid to produce hydrogen gas are above H in the activity series:

Mg, Ca, Zn, Fe, Sn

4. Rank the metals tested in acid from most to least reactive.

Considering typical reactions with acid, an approximate order is:

Ca > Mg > Zn > Fe > Sn > Pb > Cu

5. Combine the results from both parts (metal nitrates and acid) into a single activity series that also includes hydrogen.

Overall combined series (most to least reactive):

Mg > Ca > Zn > Fe > Sn > Pb > Cu

Hydrogen is located between Sn and Pb.

Comparing to the Standard Activity Series

6. How does your list compare to the usual activity series on a periodic table or reference chart?

It should agree with the common activity series: K > Ca > Na > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au. The metals used in the lab fit in at the same relative positions, confirming the trend.

Why Lead Sometimes Appears Not to React

7. Lead does not seem to react in acid in the lab, but according to the activity series it should. Why?

Lead can start to react with acid, but it forms an insoluble coating of compounds like PbSO₄ or PbCl₂ on its surface. This layer blocks further contact between the metal and the acid, so bubbling stops and it appears that no reaction is occurring.

Molecular Explanation of Reactivity: Ca vs. Mg vs. Na

8. On a molecular level, why do Ca, Mg, and Na show different reactivities?

Reactivity of metals depends on how easily they lose valence electrons.

  • Calcium (Ca): Has two valence electrons and relatively low ionization energy in its group. It readily loses electrons to form Ca²⁺, so it reacts very vigorously with acid.
  • Magnesium (Mg): Also forms Mg²⁺ but has a slightly higher ionization energy than Ca, so it reacts strongly but somewhat less violently.
  • Sodium (Na): Has one valence electron and is extremely reactive with water. In typical solid-metal/acid setups, its behaviour differs because of how it is handled, but in general Na’s very low ionization energy makes it highly reactive.

In summary, lower ionization energy and easier loss of electrons mean higher metallic reactivity.
