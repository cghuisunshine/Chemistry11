Chemical Bonding Models

Valence Bond Theory (VBT) — A Detailed Tutorial

VBT explains covalent bonds as overlap of atomic orbitals that hold a pair of electrons. In Chemistry 11, you can use VBT to connect Lewis structures and VSEPR shapes to the idea of hybrid orbitals, and to understand single / double / triple bonds as σ and π bonds.

The Big Idea Why bonds form

Valence Bond Theory says a covalent bond forms when two half-filled orbitals overlap and the electrons pair up with opposite spin. Overlap concentrates electron density between nuclei, which lowers potential energy.

What VBT helps you explain

  • Why single bonds are usually σ (sigma) bonds.
  • Why double and triple bonds include π (pi) bonds.
  • Why molecules have specific 3D shapes (via hybrid orbitals).
  • Why a bond has a preferred direction (orbital overlap is directional).

What you still use alongside VBT

  • Lewis structures + VSEPR to count electron groups and predict shape.
  • Electronegativity to predict polarity and bond type.
  • Formal charge/resonance for best Lewis structure (VBT can struggle with delocalization).
In Chem 11, VBT is usually taught as a model: it’s not the only way to describe bonding, but it’s very useful for shapes and σ/π counting.

Orbitals Refresher s and p

An orbital is a region of space where an electron is likely to be found. Orbitals have shapes that matter for overlap.

Key facts you need

  • s orbital: spherical; one per energy level (2 e⁻ max).
  • p orbitals: dumbbell-shaped; three orientations (px, py, pz); each holds 2 e⁻.
  • Unpaired electrons are “available” for bonding; paired electrons usually show up as lone pairs.
Mini-check: why do we talk about “valence” orbitals?

Bonding happens using the outer (valence) electrons because they are the least tightly held. For main-group elements, that’s usually the ns and np orbitals.

Orbital Overlap → σ and π Bonds single/double/triple

VBT pictures a bond as overlap between orbitals on different atoms.

σ (sigma) bonds

  • Form by end-to-end overlap (along the internuclear axis).
  • Electron density is centered on the line between the nuclei.
  • All single bonds are σ bonds.

π (pi) bonds

  • Form by side-by-side overlap of two parallel p orbitals.
  • Electron density is above and below (or left/right of) the bond axis.
  • A π bond requires unhybridized p orbitals to be available.
This is a simplified picture (real orbitals are 3D probability clouds), but it captures the key idea: σ overlap points along the bond axis; π overlap sits above/below it.
Quick counting rule: σ/π in multiple bonds 
Single bond: 1 σ
Double bond: 1 σ + 1 π
Triple bond: 1 σ + 2 π

Hybridization sp, sp², sp³

Hybridization is a model where an atom “mixes” its valence orbitals to make new orbitals that point in the directions needed for bonding.

Hybridization is not something atoms literally “do” like a chemical reaction. It’s a useful way to describe observed shapes and bonding patterns.

The Chem 11 mapping (most common)

Electron groups* Hybridization Electron geometry Ideal angles Notes
2 sp Linear 180° Leaves 2 unhybridized p orbitals for π bonding
3 sp2 Trigonal planar 120° Leaves 1 unhybridized p orbital for 1 π bond
4 sp3 Tetrahedral 109.5° No p orbitals left (so usually only single bonds)

*Electron groups = σ bonds + lone pairs around the central atom. (A double bond counts as 1 group in VSEPR and for this mapping.)

Extension: expanded octets (beyond typical Chem 11)

Some molecules are described with 5 or 6 electron groups (e.g., PCl₅, SF₆). You might see labels like dsp³ and d²sp³ in older textbooks. Modern treatments are more nuanced, but for shape prediction you still use VSEPR: 5 groups → trigonal bipyramidal, 6 groups → octahedral.

Step-by-Step Method (Chem 11) Do this every time

  1. Draw the Lewis structure (including any double/triple bonds and lone pairs).
  2. Choose the atom you are describing (often the central atom).
  3. Count electron groups around that atom:
    • Each single bond = 1 group (σ bond).
    • Each double bond = 1 group (still just one region of electron density).
    • Each triple bond = 1 group.
    • Each lone pair = 1 group.
  4. Map groups → hybridization (2 → sp, 3 → sp², 4 → sp³).
  5. Use VSEPR for shape (electron geometry from groups; molecular geometry from bonding vs lone pairs).
  6. Count σ and π bonds in the whole molecule if asked.
Why does a double bond count as 1 group?

VSEPR (and this hybridization shortcut) cares about regions of electron density. A double bond has more electrons, but they are in the same region between the same two atoms, so it behaves like one “group” for geometry.

Worked Examples Show your method

Example 1: CH₄ (methane)

Lewis: C has 4 single bonds to H; no lone pairs on C.

  • Electron groups around C: 4 σ bonds → 4 groups
  • Hybridization: sp³
  • Electron geometry: tetrahedral
  • Molecular geometry: tetrahedral
  • Bond angles: ~109.5°
CH4: 4 groups → sp3 → tetrahedral → 109.5°
Example 2: NH₃ (ammonia)

Lewis: N has 3 single bonds + 1 lone pair.

  • Electron groups around N: 3 σ + 1 lone pair → 4 groups
  • Hybridization: sp³
  • Electron geometry: tetrahedral
  • Molecular geometry: trigonal pyramidal (AX₃E)
  • Angles: slightly less than 109.5° (about 107°)
Example 3: H₂O (water)

Lewis: O has 2 single bonds + 2 lone pairs.

  • Electron groups around O: 2 σ + 2 lone pairs → 4 groups
  • Hybridization: sp³
  • Electron geometry: tetrahedral
  • Molecular geometry: bent (AX₂E₂)
  • Angles: ~104.5° (smaller due to lone pairs)
Same hybridization (sp³) as CH₄ and NH₃, but different molecular geometry because of different lone-pair counts.
Example 4: CO₂ (carbon dioxide)

Lewis: O=C=O (two double bonds), no lone pairs on C.

  • Electron groups around C: 2 (each double bond counts as 1 group)
  • Hybridization: sp
  • Geometry: linear, 180°
  • σ/π: each C=O has 1 σ + 1 π → total 2 σ and 2 π in the molecule
CO2: 2 groups → sp → linear; total bonds = 2 σ + 2 π
Example 5: C₂H₄ (ethene) — a double bond

Lewis: H₂C=CH₂ (one C=C double bond).

  • Focus on one carbon: it has 3 electron groups (two C–H σ bonds + one C–C σ bond)
  • Hybridization on each carbon: sp²
  • Geometry around each carbon: trigonal planar (~120°); molecule is planar near the double bond
  • The π bond comes from the unhybridized p orbital on each carbon overlapping sideways
C2H4: each C has 3 groups → sp2; total = 5 σ + 1 π
Example 6: C₂H₂ (ethyne) — a triple bond

Lewis: H–C≡C–H.

  • Each carbon has 2 electron groups (one C–H σ bond, one C–C σ bond)
  • Hybridization on each carbon: sp
  • Geometry: linear near the triple bond (180°)
  • The triple bond = 1 σ + 2 π (two sets of sideways p–p overlap)
C2H2: each C has 2 groups → sp; total = 3 σ + 2 π
Example 7: BF₃ (boron trifluoride)

Lewis: B has 3 single bonds, no lone pairs (in the simplest Lewis picture).

  • Electron groups around B: 3 → sp²
  • Geometry: trigonal planar (120°)
  • Note: B often has an incomplete octet (6 electrons) in the Lewis structure.
Chem 11 focus: shape and electron group counting. Deeper bonding in BF₃ can involve electron donation/acceptance ideas.

How VBT Connects to Lewis & VSEPR What each model answers

Model Main question What you actually do
Lewis How are valence electrons arranged? Draw bonds/lone pairs; check octet/charges.
VSEPR What is the 3D shape? Count electron groups → predict geometry/angles.
VBT How do bonds form and point in space? Describe overlap; use hybridization to match geometry; count σ/π.
Limits (good to know): Simple VBT does not naturally explain delocalized bonding (like resonance in benzene) or magnetic behavior in some molecules. More advanced models (like Molecular Orbital Theory) handle those better.

Practice Set Try first, then open answers

  1. Determine the hybridization and molecular geometry of the central atom in SO₂.
  2. How many σ and π bonds are in CO (carbon monoxide) if you treat it as a triple bond?
  3. For HCN, give: (a) hybridization on C, (b) geometry about C, (c) total σ and π bonds.
  4. For C₂H₆ (ethane), what is the hybridization on each carbon? How many π bonds?
  5. For C₂H₄, why is rotation around the C=C bond restricted?
  6. Predict the electron-group geometry and molecular geometry of ClF₃ (5 electron groups case).
  7. For NO₃⁻ (nitrate), what challenge does resonance create for a simple “one π bond” picture?
  8. Pick one: explain why NH₃ has a smaller bond angle than CH₄, using electron pair repulsion ideas.
Answers (open after you try)
  1. SO₂: S has 3 electron groups (2 bonding regions + 1 lone pair) → sp²; electron geometry trigonal planar; molecular geometry bent.
  2. CO triple bond model: 1 σ + 2 π.
  3. HCN: C has 2 groups (H–C and C≡N) → sp, linear at C; total bonds = 2 σ (H–C and C–N σ) + 2 π (in the triple bond) → 2 π.
  4. C₂H₆: each C has 4 groups → sp³; π bonds = 0.
  5. C₂H₄ rotation: the π bond comes from side-by-side overlap; rotation would break the parallel alignment and destroy the π overlap, so rotation is restricted.
  6. ClF₃: 5 electron groups (3 bonds + 2 lone pairs) → VSEPR electron geometry trigonal bipyramidal; lone pairs go equatorial → molecular geometry T-shaped.
  7. NO₃⁻ resonance: the “double bond” is not fixed to one N–O; electron density is delocalized across all three N–O bonds, so simple localized π counting is incomplete.
  8. Angles: lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles (NH₃ ~107° vs CH₄ 109.5°).

Common Mistakes Avoid these

  • Counting a double bond as 2 electron groups (it’s 1 region)
  • Forgetting lone pairs when choosing hybridization
  • Mixing electron geometry (groups) with molecular geometry (atoms)
  • Assuming sp³ means the molecule is always tetrahedral (lone pairs change shape)
  • Thinking a π bond can form without leftover p orbitals
