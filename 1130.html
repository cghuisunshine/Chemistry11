7. Trends & Explanations

Electron configuration down a column

Answer
Elements in the same column have the same pattern of valence electrons, but each step down adds a higher principal energy level n (for example: Li [He] 2s¹, Na [Ne] 3s¹, K [Ar] 4s¹).

Density trends

Down a column
For many groups, especially metals, density generally increases down a column because mass increases faster than atomic volume.
Across a row of metals
Density usually rises toward the middle of the row (transition metals) and may decrease again toward the right.

Placing elements in the table

Which column?
The number of valence electrons and overall chemical behaviour are the most useful clues for choosing the correct column (group).
Which row?
Use trends in radius, density, and ionization energy down the group: radius increases, density often increases, and ionization energy decreases.

Atomic radius trends

Down a column
Atomic radius increases as you move down a group because each step adds another energy level and more shielding.
Across a row
Atomic radius decreases from left to right across a period as the number of protons increases, pulling electrons in the same shell closer.

Ion formation

Metals
Metals lose one or more valence electrons to form positive ions (cations) with smaller radii than the neutral atoms.
Non-metals
Non-metals gain electrons to form negative ions (anions) with larger radii than the neutral atoms.

Ionization energy trends

Down a column
Ionization energy generally decreases because outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded.
Across a row
Ionization energy generally increases because nuclear charge increases while shielding changes little, so valence electrons are held more tightly.

Exceptions to ionization-energy trend

Examples
Be vs B (and Mg vs Al) when starting a new p-subshell, and N vs O (and P vs S) when electrons pair up in a p-orbital, create small dips in the overall increasing trend across a period.

Explaining density, radius, and IE together

Row 4 density
Across row 4, mass increases while radius usually decreases, so more mass is packed into less volume. This gives high densities, especially in the middle transition metals.
Atomic radius (protons & electrons)
Across a period, more protons pull electrons in the same energy level closer → radius decreases. Down a group, added energy levels and shielding push electrons farther out → radius increases.
Ionization energy (protons & electrons)
Across a period, more protons with similar shielding make electrons harder to remove → IE increases. Down a group, electrons are farther and more shielded → IE decreases.
