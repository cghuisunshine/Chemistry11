Goal: explain how the periodic table predicts properties using electron configuration, periodic trends, bonding, shape, polarity, and intermolecular forces.

Big Idea

The periodic table is a predictive map. Patterns exist because electron structures repeat in a regular way. If you know an element’s position, you can predict its behavior.

The causal chain (memorize this)

electron configuration → periodic trends → bonding → shape → polarity → intermolecular forces → observable properties
Exam mindset Don’t memorize arrows. Justify each step using: shielding Zeff distance electron repulsion

6.1 Development of the Periodic Table

What “periodic” means

Periodic means chemical/physical properties repeat in a regular pattern. Elements with similar properties cluster into groups (columns).

Historical milestones (why the modern table exists)

  • Dobereiner’s triads: sets of 3 elements with similar properties (early grouping idea).
  • Newlands’ law of octaves: repeating pattern every 8 elements (pattern noticed, not perfect).
  • Mendeleev: arranged by mass + properties; left gaps; predicted unknown elements accurately.
  • Moseley: corrected the “key” to atomic number (protons), creating the modern table.

Modern Periodic Law

When elements are arranged by increasing atomic number (Z),
their properties recur periodically.

Why atomic number matters: atomic number fixes electron count (for neutral atoms), which fixes electron configuration, which explains chemical behavior. That’s why chemistry repeats in patterns.

Core rule Elements in the same group have similar chemistry mainly because they have similar valence-electron structures.

6.2 Periodic Trends

Periodic trends are not random. They follow from how strongly the nucleus attracts valence electrons and how electrons shield each other.

The two master factors

Factor Meaning What it changes
Shielding Inner electrons reduce nuclear attraction felt by valence electrons More shielding → outer electrons held less tightly
Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) Net pull on valence electrons Higher Zeff → smaller radius, higher IE, higher EN 
Across a period: Zeff ↑ (shielding ~ constant) → radius ↓, IE ↑, EN ↑
Down a group: n ↑ + shielding ↑ → radius ↑, IE ↓, EN ↓

Atomic radius (size)

  • Down a group: radius increases (more energy levels, bigger electron cloud).
  • Across a period: radius decreases (Zeff increases, electrons pulled closer).

Ion size

  • Cations are smaller than their atoms (loss of e⁻ → less repulsion; stronger pull per electron).
  • Anions are larger than their atoms (gain of e⁻ → more repulsion; expanded cloud).
  • Isoelectronic series: same electrons → more protons = smaller ion.

Ionization energy (IE)

Ionization energy:
Energy required to remove one electron from a gaseous atom.
  • Across a period: IE increases (Zeff increases).
  • Down a group: IE decreases (valence electrons farther + more shielding).
Important nuance Small “exceptions” to the smooth IE trend happen due to subshell stability and electron pairing repulsion (e.g., N vs O).

Electronegativity (EN)

Electronegativity:
Ability of a bonded atom to attract shared electrons.
  • Across a period: EN increases (Zeff ↑, radius ↓).
  • Down a group: EN decreases (bonding electrons farther away).
  • Highest EN: Fluorine.

ΔEN and bond character (guideline)

ΔEN Bond type (mostly) Meaning
≈ 0 Non-polar covalent Equal sharing
0.4 – 1.7 Polar covalent Unequal sharing
> 1.7 Ionic (dominant) Transfer dominates

6.3 Describing Chemical Bonding

Bond types (fast classification)

Elements involved Bond type Main idea
Metal + non-metal Ionic Electron transfer → ions attract in a lattice
Non-metal + non-metal Covalent Shared electron pairs between nuclei
Metal + metal Metallic “Sea of electrons” around positive metal ions

Ionic bonding

An ionic bond is electrostatic attraction between a cation and an anion formed by electron transfer. Ionic solids form a crystal lattice, not individual molecules.

Covalent bonding

A covalent bond is attraction between shared electron pairs and the nuclei of the bonded atoms. Sharing may be equal (non-polar) or unequal (polar), depending on ΔEN.

Bond order and bond strength

Bond order ↑ → bond length ↓ and bond energy ↑

6.4 Lewis Structure Diagrams

What Lewis structures show

  • Valence electrons
  • Bonds (shared pairs) and lone pairs
  • Electron “accounting” (not 3D shape)

Lewis algorithm (step-by-step)

1) Count total valence e− (add for − charge, subtract for + charge)
2) Choose skeleton (least EN usually central; H never central)
3) Connect atoms with single bonds (2 e− each)
4) Complete octets for outer atoms
5) Put leftover e− on central atom
6) If central lacks octet, create multiple bonds
7) Check total e− count + formal charges

Octet rule exceptions

  • Incomplete octet: Be (4), B/Al (6) common
  • Expanded octet: period 3+ atoms can exceed 8
  • Odd-electron species: radicals (rare at this level)

Resonance

Resonance occurs when more than one valid Lewis structure exists. The real molecule is a resonance hybrid with delocalized electrons. 

Resonance rules:
• Only electrons move (π bonds/lone pairs), not atoms
• Total valence electrons stay the same
• The real structure is an average (hybrid), not “switching” back and forth

6.5 Molecular Shape and Polarity (VSEPR)

Core principle

VSEPR:
Electron groups repel and arrange themselves to minimize repulsion.
Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs.

Counting electron groups (the key skill)

  • Each bond (single/double/triple) counts as one electron group.
  • Each lone pair counts as one electron group.
  • Electron-group count determines geometry; lone pairs affect the final molecular shape.

Common shapes (Chem 11 essentials)

Electron groups Typical notation Shape Example
2 AX₂ Linear CO₂
3 AX₃ / AX₂E Trigonal planar / Bent BF₃ / SO₂
4 AX₄ / AX₃E / AX₂E₂ Tetrahedral / Trig pyramidal / Bent CH₄ / NH₃ / H₂O
5 AX₅ Trigonal bipyramidal PCl₅
6 AX₆ Octahedral SF₆

Molecular polarity

A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds and the bond dipoles do not cancel. Symmetric molecules may be non-polar even if bonds are polar. 

Polarity checklist:
1) Are there polar bonds? (ΔEN > ~0.4)
2) Is the shape symmetric?
→ If not symmetric, dipoles don’t cancel → polar molecule
Example CO₂ has polar C=O bonds, but it is linear and symmetric, so it is non-polar overall.

Intermolecular Forces (IMF)

What IMFs control

  • Boiling point / melting point
  • Viscosity (thickness)
  • Volatility (evaporates easily)
  • Solubility (“like dissolves like”)

Strength ladder

Dispersion < Dipole–dipole < Hydrogen bonding < Ion–dipole

IMF types

Force Applies to Cause
Dispersion (London) All molecules/atoms Temporary induced dipoles (more with larger molar mass / surface area)
Dipole–dipole Polar molecules Permanent dipole attraction
Hydrogen bonding H–N, H–O, H–F Very strong dipole–dipole (H attached to N/O/F)
Ion–dipole Ions + polar solvent Ion attracts partial charges on a polar molecule
Solubility rule Polar dissolves polar; non-polar dissolves non-polar. Ionic compounds often dissolve in polar solvents because of ion–dipole forces.

Master Summary (Exam-Level)

If you get stuck, return to the chain and explain each link: 

electron configuration → periodic trends → bonding → shape → polarity → IMF → properties

One-sentence “big takeaway”

The periodic table works because atomic number determines electron arrangement, and electron arrangement determines the forces that control chemistry.
