7.1 Nature of Solutions

Key ideas

  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture of a solute dissolved in a solvent. It is uniform throughout (single phase).
  • Solute vs. Solvent: The solute is the substance being dissolved (minor component). The solvent is the medium (major component, usually water).
  • Electrolytes: Solutions that conduct electricity due to the presence of free-moving ions (e.g., NaCl). Non-electrolytes (e.g., Sugar) do not conduct.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Concentration (Molarity): c = n / V. Matches moles of solute (n) to liters of solution (V). Unit is M (mol/L).

Short example(s)

  • Dissolving 0.5 mol NaCl in 1.0 L water gives a 0.5 M solution.
  • Air is a gaseous solution (Nitrogen solvent, Oxygen solute).

7.2 Solubility Principles

Key ideas

  • "Like Dissolves Like": Solutes dissolve best in solvents with similar polarity.
  • Polar: Dissolves polar and ionic solutes (e.g., Water dissolves Salt).
  • Non-polar: Dissolves non-polar solutes (e.g., Benzene dissolves Grease).
  • Saturation: The point where no more solute can dissolve at a given temperature.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Dilution Formula: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂. Used when adding water to a stock solution. Moles (n) remain constant.

Short example(s)

  • Miscible: Ethanol and Water mix in all proportions (both polar).
  • Immiscible: Oil and Water separate (non-polar vs. polar).

7.3 Dissociation & Conductivity

Key ideas

  • Dissociation: The separation of ionic compounds into ions when dissolved in water.
  • Ion Charge Balance: Total positive charge must equal total negative charge.
  • Conductivity: Depends on ion concentration. More ions = Higher conductivity.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Dissociation Equation: AxBy(s) → xA⁺(aq) + yB⁻(aq). Shows the ratio of ions produced.

Short example(s)

  • CaCl₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻. 1 mole of salt produces 3 moles of ions. Highly conductive.
  • C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ (s) → C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ (aq) (Sugar). No ions produced. Non-conductive.

7.4 Solution Stoichiometry (Titrations)

Key ideas

  • Titration: A technique to determine the unknown concentration of a solution using a standard solution of known concentration.
  • Equivalence Point: The theoretical point where moles of acid exactly equal moles of base (stoichiometrically).
  • Endpoint: The experimental point where the indicator changes color.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Titration Calculation: n(acid) / coeff = n(base) / coeff. Use n = cV to solve for unknown.

Short example(s)

  • Neutralizing HCl with NaOH: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (1:1 ratio).
  • If 20 mL of 0.1M NaOH neutralizes 10 mL of HCl, then calculations show [HCl] = 0.2M.
