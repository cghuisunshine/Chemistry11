Big Idea

Most chemical reactions important to life and industry happen in solution. A solution is a homogeneous mixture where one substance (solute) is dissolved in another (solvent). The properties of the solution depend on the intermolecular forces between the particles.

Key Concept Understanding solutions tells us why oil and water don't mix, how soap works, and how to measure the exact concentration of an acid.

7.1 The Nature of Solutions

Core Definitions

  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture (looks uniform throughout, single phase). Can be solid, liquid, or gas.
  • Solute: The substance being dissolved (usually the smaller amount).
  • Solvent: The substance doing the dissolving (usually the larger amount).
  • Miscible: Two liquids that mix in any proportion (e.g., ethanol & water).
  • Immiscible: Two liquids that do not mix (e.g., oil & water).
  • Aqueous: A solution where the solvent is water.

Solubility

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. 

Common units:
• Molarity (M): mol/L (Molar Solubility)
• g/L: grams per litre
• ppm: parts per million
Calculation Tip To convert Solubility (g/L) ↔ Molar Solubility (mol/L), use the Molar Mass of the solute.
mol/L = (g/L) ÷ (g/mol)

Saturation Levels

Level Description
Unsaturated Solvent can dissolve more solute.
Saturated Solvent holds the maximum amount of solute possible. Any extra solid remains undissolved.
Supersaturated Holds more than the normal maximum (unstable condition, usually achieved by heating then cooling).

7.2 Like Dissolves Like

This simple rule predicts solubility: “Polar dissolves polar (and ionic); Non-polar dissolves non-polar.”

The Mechanism (Intermolecular Forces)

For a solute to dissolve: 1. Solute particles separate (requires energy). 2. Solvent particles separate (requires energy). 3. Solute and solvent attract each other (releases energy).

Dissolving happens when the new solute-solvent attraction is similar in strength and type to the original attractions.

Common Interactions

Solute Type Solvent Type Force Involved Result
Ionic (e.g., NaCl) Polar (Water) Ion-Dipole Soluble (Water molecules hydrate the ions)
Polar (e.g., Ethanol, Sugar) Polar (Water) Hydrogen Bonding or Dipole-Dipole Soluble (Miscible)
Non-polar (e.g., Iodine I₂) Non-polar (e.g., CCl₄, Paint Thinner) London Dispersion Soluble
Non-polar (e.g., Grease) Polar (Water) (None / Mismatch) Insoluble

Applications

  • Vitamins: Vitamin A (non-polar hydrocarbon chain) is fat-soluble (stored in body fat). Vitamin C (many -OH groups, polar) is water-soluble (needs daily intake).
  • Soaps: Soap molecules have a polar head (hydrophilic) and a non-polar tail (hydrophobic). They form micelles to trap grease (non-polar) inside while the outside interacts with water, allowing grease to be washed away.

7.3 Dissociation & Conductivity

Dilution

Adding water lowers concentration but the moles of solute stay the same. 

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
Where:
• M₁ = initial concentration
• V₁ = initial volume
• M₂ = final concentration
• V₂ = final volume (V₁ + added water)

Dissociation Equations

Ionic compounds break into ions in water. Check formulas and balance charges!

Example CaCl₂(aq) → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq)
If [CaCl₂] = 1.0 M, then:
[Ca²⁺] = 1.0 M
[Cl⁻] = 2.0 M (twice the concentration because of the coefficient 2)

Conductivity

For a solution to conduct electricity, it must contain mobile ions.

Type Examples Conductivity
Strong Electrolyte Soluble salts, Strong Acids (HCl), Strong Bases (NaOH) High (Many ions)
Weak Electrolyte Weak Acids (Vinegar/CH₃COOH), Weak Bases (NH₃) Low (Few ions, mostly molecules)
Nonelectrolyte Molecular compounds (Sugar, Alcohol) None (No ions)

7.4 Titrations

Titration is a technique to determine the unknown concentration of a solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (standard solution).

The Setup

  • Burette: Vertical tube with a tap. Holds the standard solution (titrant). Measures volume added precisely.
  • Pipette: Used to measure a precise volume of the unknown solution into the flask.
  • Erlenmeyer Flask: Contains the unknown + Indicator.

Key Terms

  • Equivalence Point: When moles of H⁺ exactly equal moles of OH⁻. (Stoichiometric match).
  • Endpoint: When the indicator changes color (experimental estimate of equivalence).

Calculation Steps

  1. Write the balanced neutralization equation (Acid + Base → Salt + Water).
  2. Calculate moles of the known substance: n = M × V.
  3. Use the mole ratio from the equation to find moles of the unknown.
  4. Calculate concentration of the unknown: M = n / V.
Common Mistake Don't forget the mole ratio! If the reaction is H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH, you need 2 moles of base for every 1 mole of acid.

Master Summary

Solution chemistry connects the microscopic (intermolecular forces, ions) to the macroscopic (solubility, conductivity, titration results). 

Key Logic Chain:
• Molecular Polarity → Solubility (Like Dissolves Like)
• Dissociation → Ions → Conductivity
• Moles & Stoichiometry → Titration Calculations
