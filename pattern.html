6.1

Periodic Table & Patterns structure & organization

How electron configuration controls an element‚Äôs position & properties.

Roots Core ideas & definitions
‚ñ∂

Periodic law: When elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number, their physical and chemical properties repeat in a regular, periodic pattern.

Period: Horizontal row (1‚Äì7). Each new period means a new main energy level (shell) is being filled.

Group / family: Vertical column (1‚Äì18). Elements in a group have similar outer (valence) electron configurations and therefore similar chemical behaviour.

  • Main-group (representative) elements: Groups 1‚Äì2, 13‚Äì18.
  • Transition elements: Groups 3‚Äì12 (d-block).
  • Inner transition elements: Lanthanides & actinides (f-block).

Blocks: s-block, p-block, d-block, f-block show which sublevel is being filled. The order of blocks across each period matches the Aufbau order for filling sublevels.

The two most important facts:
1) Elements in the same family have similar properties because they have similar valence electron configurations.
2) As you move down a family or across a period, many properties change in regular periodic trends.

Trunk Rules & patterns
‚ñ∂
  • Atoms in the same group share the same number of valence electrons (for main-group elements), which explains repeated patterns in reactivity, ion formation, and bonding.
  • Each new period begins when you start filling a new principal energy level‚Äôs s-sublevel.
  • The position of an element in the table is a map of its electron configuration: s-block (left), d-block (middle), p-block (right), f-block (bottom).
  • The fact that quantum mechanics predicts electron configurations that match the periodic table‚Äôs pattern is strong evidence that the periodic table reflects the true structure of atoms.
Leaves Examples & interpretations
‚ñ∂

Example ‚Äì Similar family behaviour: Na and K are both in group 1. Their valence configurations end in ns¬π, so both form +1 ions (Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫) and react vigorously with water.

Reading positions like a map: An element is in period 3, group 16 (S, sulphur) ‚Äì this tells you it has 3 main energy levels and 6 valence electrons.

Teaching tip: Have students pick an element, write its electron configuration, then explain how its position (period, group, block) reflects that configuration.

6.2

Periodic Trends size ¬∑ IE ¬∑ EN

How atomic radius, ionization energy & electronegativity change across the periodic table.

Roots Core definitions
‚ñ∂

Atomic radius: Distance from the nucleus to the boundary of the surrounding electron cloud; a measure of how ‚Äúbig‚Äù the atom is.

First ionization energy (IE‚ÇÅ): Energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous neutral atom.

Electronegativity (EN): Relative ability of a bonded atom to attract the shared electrons toward itself. It is a unitless scale (Pauling scale: ~0.7 to 4.0).

Shielding: Inner (core) electrons reduce the effective pull of the nucleus on outer electrons.

Trunk Trends & explanations
‚ñ∂

Atomic radius trends:

  • Down a group: radius increases. More energy levels and more inner electrons ‚Üí more shielding and a bigger electron cloud.
  • Across a period (left ‚Üí right): radius decreases. Protons are added to the same shell; shielding doesn‚Äôt increase much, so outer electrons feel a stronger pull and are drawn closer.
  • Largest atoms: near the bottom left. Smallest atoms: near the top right.

Ionization energy trends:

  • Across a period: IE increases. Atoms get smaller and electrons are held more tightly.
  • Down a group: IE decreases. Valence electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded, so easier to remove.
  • Metals (left) have low IE; non-metals (right) have high IE.
  • Second IE is much higher after a stable shell has formed (e.g. Li‚Äôs IE‚ÇÇ is >10√ó IE‚ÇÅ).

Electronegativity trends:

  • Across a period: EN increases (atoms become smaller and nuclear attraction is stronger).
  • Down a group: EN decreases (atoms are larger and more shielded).
  • Most electronegative: fluorine (EN ‚âà 4.0). Least: large alkali metals like cesium (EN ‚âà 0.7).

Cations vs anions: Cations (positive ions) are always smaller than their neutral atoms; anions (negative ions) are always larger due to increased electron-electron repulsion.

Leaves Examples, tips & comparisons
‚ñ∂

Example 1 ‚Äì Ordering atomic radii in a period: In period 3 (Na ‚Üí Ar), size decreases from left to right: Na > Mg > Al > Si > P > S > Cl > Ar.

Example 2 ‚Äì Ordering first ionization energies: For the same set, IE increases roughly Na < Mg < Al < Si < P < S < Cl < Ar.

Concept check: Elements with low IE and EN tend to lose electrons in reactions (forming cations); elements with high IE and EN tend to gain or share electrons (non-metals).

6.3

Describing Chemical Bonding ionic ¬∑ covalent ¬∑ ŒîEN

How electronegativity differences explain ionic, polar covalent & non-polar bonds.

Roots Valence electrons & bond types
‚ñ∂

Atoms bond to reach a lower potential energy, often by forming a stable octet (8 valence electrons) like noble gases.

Ionic bond: Electrostatic attraction between cations and anions that forms when a metal transfers electron(s) to a non-metal.

Covalent bond: Bond formed by sharing one or more pairs of electrons between two atoms, usually two non-metals.

Metallic bond: Bonding between metals where valence electrons move through a ‚Äúsea of electrons‚Äù shared by many positive metal ions (not deeply covered in this unit).

Trunk ŒîEN rules & bond character
‚ñ∂

We use the difference in electronegativity to estimate bond type:

  • 0 < ŒîEN < 0.4 ‚Üí Mostly covalent. Almost equal sharing, very little ionic character.
  • 0.4 ‚â≤ ŒîEN ‚â≤ 1.7 ‚Üí Polar covalent. Unequal sharing, bond dipole forms.
  • ŒîEN ‚â≥ 1.7 ‚Üí Mostly ionic. Electron effectively transferred; ionic crystal lattice forms.

Bond dipole: In a polar covalent bond, electron density is pulled toward the more electronegative atom, giving that end a partial negative charge (Œ¥‚Åª) and the other end Œ¥‚Å∫.

Ionic compounds: High melting points, form extended 3D lattices (e.g., NaCl). Their formulas show a ratio of ions, not a single molecule.

Covalent compounds: Often exist as independent molecules (e.g., H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ), sometimes as giant covalent networks (diamond, quartz) with very high melting points.

6.4

Lewis Structures octet rule & bonding patterns

Using valence electrons to draw 2D electron-dot diagrams for molecules & ions.

Roots Octet rule & electron dots
‚ñ∂

Octet rule: Main-group atoms tend to lose, gain, or share electrons until they have 8 valence electrons like a noble gas.

Lewis structure: 2D diagram showing valence electrons as dots and shared pairs as lines between atoms.

Valence electrons: Outer-shell electrons involved in bonding. For main-group elements, usually given by the group number (e.g., group 16 ‚Üí 6 valence e‚Åª).

Special cases: H only needs 2 electrons (duet rule), and some atoms can have incomplete or expanded octets.

Trunk Drawing rules & exceptions
‚ñ∂

General steps for drawing a Lewis structure:

  1. Count total valence electrons for all atoms.
  2. Choose a central atom (usually the one that needs the most electrons or is least electronegative, not H).
  3. Connect outer atoms to the central atom with single bonds (each line = 2 electrons).
  4. Complete octets for outer atoms using lone pairs.
  5. Place any remaining electrons as lone pairs on the central atom.
  6. If the central atom has less than 8 electrons, form double or triple bonds by converting lone pairs on neighbouring atoms into shared pairs.
  7. Check that the total number of electrons is correct and that octets are satisfied (or explain exceptions).

Important exceptions:

  • Incomplete octet: B, Be, Al and a few others can be stable with fewer than 8 electrons.
  • Expanded octet: Elements in period 3 or beyond (P, S, Cl, Br, Xe) can have more than 8 valence electrons.
  • Odd-electron species: Radicals with an odd total number of electrons (not emphasized in this course).

Lewis structures provide the starting point for 3D shapes in VSEPR: each bond and lone pair is one ‚Äúelectron group‚Äù around the central atom.

Leaves Worked examples & tips
‚ñ∂

Example ‚Äì CO‚ÇÇ: C has 4 valence e‚Åª; each O has 6. Total = 16 e‚Åª. Skeleton: O‚ÄìC‚ÄìO. Add lone pairs to O atoms, then form two double bonds to give O=C=O, with each atom having an octet.

Example ‚Äì H‚ÇÇO: O has 6 valence e‚Åª; each H has 1. Total = 8 e‚Åª. Skeleton: H‚ÄìO‚ÄìH. Two lone pairs go on O, giving each atom a full shell (H has 2, O has 8).

Example ‚Äì NH‚ÇÉ: N has 5 valence e‚Åª; H has 1 each. Total = 8 e‚Åª. Skeleton: three N‚ÄìH bonds plus one lone pair on N. This leads to a trigonal pyramidal shape in VSEPR.

Teaching tip: Let students build 3D clay-and-stick models for molecules like CO‚ÇÇ, NH‚ÇÉ, and H‚ÇÇO to see how electron pairs spread out in space.

6.5

VSEPR & Molecular Polarity AX notation ¬∑ shape ¬∑ dipole

Predicting 3D shapes and deciding whether molecules are polar or non-polar.

Roots VSEPR basics & AXE notation
‚ñ∂

VSEPR theory: Valence-shell electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) repel each other and arrange in 3D so they are as far apart as possible, giving the molecule a characteristic shape.

Each single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair counts as one electron group or domain.

We describe the central atom with AXE notation: A = central atom, X = number of bonded atoms, E = number of lone pairs on A.

  • AX‚ÇÇ ‚Üí linear (e.g., CO‚ÇÇ).
  • AX‚ÇÉ ‚Üí trigonal planar; AX‚ÇÇE ‚Üí bent (3 electron groups).
  • AX‚ÇÑ ‚Üí tetrahedral; AX‚ÇÉE ‚Üí trigonal pyramidal; AX‚ÇÇE‚ÇÇ ‚Üí bent (4 electron groups).
Trunk Shapes, angles & dipole rules
‚ñ∂

Three electron groups (AX‚ÇÉ / AX‚ÇÇE):

  • Electron-group geometry: trigonal planar (120¬∞).
  • AX‚ÇÉ: trigonal planar molecule (e.g., BF‚ÇÉ).
  • AX‚ÇÇE: bent/angular molecule (e.g., SO‚ÇÇ). Lone pair compresses bond angle to <120¬∞.

Four electron groups (AX‚ÇÑ / AX‚ÇÉE / AX‚ÇÇE‚ÇÇ):

  • Electron-group geometry: tetrahedral (ideal 109.5¬∞).
  • AX‚ÇÑ: tetrahedral shape (CH‚ÇÑ) with ‚âà109.5¬∞ bond angles.
  • AX‚ÇÉE: trigonal pyramidal (NH‚ÇÉ) with ‚âà107¬∞ angles (lone pair pushes bonds closer).
  • AX‚ÇÇE‚ÇÇ: bent or V-shaped (H‚ÇÇO) with ‚âà104.5¬∞ angles.

Lone-pair vs bond-pair repulsion: Lone pairs are held by one nucleus and spread out more, creating stronger repulsion than bonding pairs. This compresses adjacent bond angles.

Molecular polarity: A molecule is polar if it has bond dipoles that do not cancel, giving a net dipole moment. It is non-polar if there are no polar bonds or if the bond dipoles cancel by symmetry.

Leaves Key polarity examples
‚ñ∂

Example ‚Äì CO‚ÇÇ: AX‚ÇÇ ‚Üí linear. Two identical polar C=O bonds in opposite directions. Dipoles cancel ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ is non-polar.

Example ‚Äì H‚ÇÇO: AX‚ÇÇE‚ÇÇ ‚Üí bent. Two O‚ÄìH polar bonds with bond dipoles pointing in roughly the same direction. Dipoles reinforce ‚Üí water is strongly polar.

Example ‚Äì CCl‚ÇÑ vs CH‚ÇÉCl: Both are tetrahedral (AX‚ÇÑ). In CCl‚ÇÑ, four identical C‚ÄìCl bond dipoles cancel ‚Üí non-polar. In CH‚ÇÉCl, the three C‚ÄìH and one C‚ÄìCl dipole do not cancel ‚Üí CH‚ÇÉCl is polar.

Example ‚Äì Is NH‚ÇÉ polar? Lewis structure: three N‚ÄìH bonds and one lone pair (AX‚ÇÉE ‚Üí trigonal pyramidal). All bond dipoles point toward N and do not cancel ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ is polar.

Teaching tip: Have students decide polarity with a 3-step checklist: (1) Draw Lewis structure, (2) Identify shape with AXE notation, (3) Combine bond dipoles to see if they cancel.
