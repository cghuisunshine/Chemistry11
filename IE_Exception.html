Ionization Energy ‚Äì Mathematical Explanation

Question

Explain that mathematically: It is proportional to the magnitude of the electrostatic attraction between the electrons being removed and the nucleus, combined with the electrostatic repulsion from the remaining electrons.

Answer

Here is the mathematical explanation of the sentence:

‚ÄúIonization energy is proportional to the magnitude of the electrostatic attraction between the electron being removed and the nucleus, combined with the electrostatic repulsion from the remaining electrons.‚Äù

We express both attraction and repulsion using Coulomb‚Äôs law.

1. Electrostatic attraction (nucleus pulling the electron in)

The nucleus has charge \(+Ze\) and the electron has charge \(-e\).
By Coulomb‚Äôs law, the force of attraction is

\( F_{\text{attract}} = \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r^2} \)

The associated potential energy is

\( U_{\text{attract}} = - \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} \)

This term is negative because attraction stabilizes the electron. The ionization energy contribution is proportional to the magnitude of this stabilization:

\( IE \propto \left|U_{\text{attract}}\right| = \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} \)

So:

  • Larger \(Z\) → stronger attraction → higher ionization energy.
  • Smaller radius \(r\) → electron closer to nucleus → higher ionization energy.

2. Electron‚Äìelectron repulsion (other electrons pushing the electron out)

Each remaining electron has charge \(-e\). If there are \(n\) other electrons, their average repulsive contribution to the potential energy for the electron being removed is

\( U_{\text{repulsion}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{ei}} \)

This energy is positive because repulsion makes the electron easier to remove, so it reduces the ionization energy.

Altogether, a simple proportionality is

\( IE \propto \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} - \sum_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{ei}} \)

3. Combine attraction and repulsion: effective nuclear charge

We often summarize the effect of nuclear attraction and electron repulsion using the effective nuclear charge:

\( Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - S \)

where \(Z\) is the actual nuclear charge and \(S\) is a shielding constant that represents the average repulsion from other electrons.

The electron then ‚Äúfeels‚Äù a net potential energy

\( U_{\text{total}} = - \dfrac{Z_{\text{eff}} e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} \)

Since the ionization energy is proportional to the magnitude of this stabilization, we can write

\( IE \propto \dfrac{Z_{\text{eff}}}{r} \)

A more refined (quantum mechanical) expression for an electron in a shell with principal quantum number \(n\) is

\( IE \propto \dfrac{(Z_{\text{eff}})^2}{n^2} \)

4. Final compact expression

A compact way to express the original sentence mathematically is:

\( \boxed{ \displaystyle IE \;\propto\; \left( \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} \right) \;-\; \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{ei}} \right) } \)

The first term is the magnitude of the electrostatic attraction to the nucleus. The second term represents the net electron‚Äìelectron repulsion from the remaining electrons. Together they determine how hard it is to remove the electron, i.e., the ionization energy.

Why this explains N vs O

Oxygen has a paired 2p electron. That pairing increases electron‚Äìelectron repulsion, so the repulsion term is larger for oxygen:

\( \sum \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{ei}} \text{ is larger in O} \)

This reduces the effective nuclear charge felt by that paired electron:

\( Z_{\text{eff}}(\text{O, paired electron}) < Z_{\text{eff}}(\text{N}) \)

Consequently:

\( IE(\text{O}) < IE(\text{N}) \)

üîç Why does electron‚Äìelectron repulsion increase when electrons are paired in the same orbital?

Pauli exclusion forces a paired set into the same spatial orbital with opposite spins, so their charge clouds overlap and the Coulomb repulsion term is maximized. Step-by-step:

  1. Coulomb repulsion (classical):
    \( F = \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r^2} \),   \( U_{\text{repulsion}} \propto \dfrac{1}{r} \)
    Larger average separation \(r\) in different orbitals means weaker repulsion. Sharing one orbital shrinks \(r\) (more overlap), so the repulsion energy rises.
  2. Pauli + overlap (quantum): opposite spins are allowed only by occupying the same spatial orbital. The overlap integral grows, boosting the electron‚Äìelectron term:
    \( U_{\text{repulsion}} \sim \int \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{12}} \left|\psi(r_1)\right|^2 \left|\psi(r_2)\right|^2 \, dr_1 dr_2 \)
    Strong overlap \(\lvert \psi \rvert\) makes this integral large, so pairing costs energy.
  3. Hund's rule consequence: pairing is expensive, so electrons spread across degenerate orbitals first to minimize \( U_{\text{repulsion}} \).
  4. Applied to N vs O: N (2p\(^3\)) has three unpaired electrons in three p orbitals → minimal repulsion. O (2p\(^4\)) must pair one orbital, so \( U_{\text{repulsion}}(\text{O}) > U_{\text{repulsion}}(\text{N}) \). Removing that paired electron relieves the repulsion, so \( IE(\text{O}) < IE(\text{N}) \).

üîç Why this explains Be > B

  • B removes a 2p electron (higher energy, smaller \(Z_{\text{eff}}\)).
  • Be removes a 2s electron (lower energy, larger \(Z_{\text{eff}}\)).
\( Z_{\text{eff}}(2p \text{ in B}) < Z_{\text{eff}}(2s \text{ in Be}) \)

So the ionization energy trend follows:

\( IE(\text{B}) < IE(\text{Be}) \)

üîç Why Mg > Al

Q: Why does the first ionization energy of Mg (7.61 eV) exceed that of Al (5.95 eV)?

A: Removing Al‚Äôs 3p electron is easier than removing Mg‚Äôs 3s electron because the 3p electron sits farther out, is more shielded, and is higher in energy, so its net Coulomb attraction to the nucleus is weaker.

Using the same Coulomb balance of attraction and repulsion:

\( IE \propto \left( \dfrac{Ze^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r} \right) - \left( \sum \dfrac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r_{ei}} \right) \)

Summarize it with effective nuclear charge, where shielding reduces the attraction term:

\( IE \propto \dfrac{Z_{\text{eff}}}{r} \quad\text{with}\quad Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - S \)

Apply this to the electron being removed:

  • Mg (3s2): remove a 3s electron. 3s penetrates more → smaller \(r\); less shielding from same-shell electrons → larger \(Z_{\text{eff}}\); orbital energy is lower (more negative, more stable). Together this makes \(Z_{\text{eff}}/r\) large and the electron harder to pull off.
  • Al (3s2 3p1): remove a 3p electron. 3p is less penetrating → larger \(r\); stronger shielding from 3s and other 3p electrons → smaller \(Z_{\text{eff}}\); orbital energy is higher (less negative, less stable). Together this makes \(Z_{\text{eff}}/r\) smaller and the electron easier to remove.
\( \dfrac{Z_{\text{eff}}}{r} \bigg|_{\text{Al, 3p}} < \dfrac{Z_{\text{eff}}}{r} \bigg|_{\text{Mg, 3s}} \;\Rightarrow\; IE(\text{Al}) < IE(\text{Mg}) \)

In one sentence: a 3p electron is farther away, more shielded, and higher in energy than a 3s electron, so Al‚Äôs 3p electron feels less nuclear pull and needs less energy to remove than Mg‚Äôs 3s electron.
