Chapter 5 – A Closer Look at Matter
Quantum story of the atom from Dalton’s solid spheres to probabilistic orbitals, organized as a learning tree: roots → trunk → branches → leaves → fruit.
Tip: dbl-click bullets to copy ✂️
5.1
Early Models of the Atom — Dalton to Rutherford
From indivisible “atomos” to electrons and a tiny massive nucleus.
R Roots — Phenomena & First Ideas
Why atoms were proposed at all
  • Fixed mass ratios in reactions (Lavoisier, Proust) suggest matter is built from discrete units that combine in whole-number ratios.
  • Democritus’ “atomos” (uncuttable particles) vs Aristotle’s continuous four elements (earth, air, fire, water).
  • Alchemists fail to turn base metals into gold, but develop real techniques (distillation, acid preparation, ore processing).
  • Boyle rejects Aristotle’s four elements and defines “element” experimentally, pushing chemistry toward quantitative science.
T Trunk — Core Models
Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford
  • Dalton: atoms are indivisible particles; atoms of the same element are identical; compounds are fixed atom ratios; reactions rearrange atoms.
  • Thomson: cathode rays are tiny negative particles (electrons) present in all atoms; “plum pudding” model = electrons in a diffuse positive sphere.
  • Rutherford: gold foil experiment shows atoms are mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positive nucleus containing most of the mass.
  • Chadwick: discovers neutrons in the nucleus → explains missing mass without charge.
B Branches — Important Sub-concepts
Subatomic particles & isotopes
  • Protons: 1+ charge, ~1 amu, in nucleus; define atomic number Z and element identity.
  • Neutrons: 0 charge, ~1 amu, in nucleus; change mass number A without changing element.
  • Electrons: −1 charge, tiny mass; outside nucleus; control bonding and chemistry.
  • Isotopes: same Z, different A (different number of neutrons); nearly identical chemistry.
  • Mass spectrometry: separates isotopes by mass/charge and lets us calculate average atomic mass from abundances.
L Leaves — Facts & Skills
Numbers & notation
  • Atomic number Z = # of protons = nuclear charge; mass number A = # of protons + neutrons.
  • Neutron number N = A − Z; ions: # electrons = Z − charge (cation) or Z + |charge| (anion).
  • Isotope notation: AZX, e.g. 146C or with charge for ions, e.g. 2713Al³⁺.
  • Average atomic mass = Σ(mass isotope × fractional abundance) over all naturally occurring isotopes.
F Fruit — Big Takeaways
What 5.1 really proves
  • Atoms are not indivisible billiard balls; they have internal structure: nucleus + electrons.
  • The identity of an element is locked by proton number, not mass or charge.
  • Nearly all atomic mass is in the nucleus, but nearly all atomic volume is empty space occupied by electrons.
  • Modern atomic structure is built on experiments with rays and particles, not on philosophical speculation.
5.2
Quantum Theory & the Bohr Model of the Atom
Quantized energy levels rescue Rutherford’s collapsing atom and explain bright-line spectra.
R Roots — Phenomena
Why classical physics failed
  • Blackbody radiation: heated solids emit a non-classical spectrum; classical theory predicts “ultraviolet catastrophe”.
  • Photoelectric effect: no electrons emitted below a threshold frequency, regardless of light intensity.
  • Hydrogen emission spectrum: discrete coloured lines (red, blue-green, blue, violet) instead of a continuous rainbow.
  • Classical prediction: accelerating electrons in Rutherford orbits should radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus → atoms would collapse, which they don’t.
T Trunk — Core Theory
Planck, Einstein & Bohr
  • Planck: energy is quantized; E = hv; objects can only absorb/emit energy in whole-number packets (quanta).
  • Einstein: uses quanta to explain the photoelectric effect → light comes in photons with energy hv.
  • Bohr: electrons occupy fixed energy levels (orbits) around the nucleus; each level has a quantum number n.
  • Bohr: electrons do not radiate energy in allowed orbits; they emit/absorb photons only when jumping between levels (ΔE = hv).
B Branches — Sub-concepts
Hydrogen spectrum & series
  • Emission: electrons absorb energy, jump to higher n, then fall back and emit photons with specific energies.
  • Balmer series: transitions ending at n = 2 give visible lines (red, blue-green, blue, violet).
  • Lyman series: transitions ending at n = 1 give UV lines; Paschen series: transitions ending at n = 3 give IR lines.
  • Only certain transitions are allowed → only certain wavelengths appear → bright-line spectrum instead of a full rainbow.
Rydberg/Balmer equations and Bohr’s energy formula all encode the same idea: energy levels are discrete, not continuous.
L Leaves — Facts & Tools
Numbers & equations
  • Planck’s equation: E = hv; v = c/λ → E = hc/λ for a photon.
  • Energy-level differences in H: ΔE = b (1/n² − 1/n'²) with b ≈ 2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ J.
  • Wavelength of emitted light: 1/λ = (b/hc) (1/n² − 1/n'²) with (b/hc) ≈ 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹.
  • Example: n' = 4 → n = 2 transition in H gives λ ≈ 486 nm (blue-green line).
F Fruit — Big Takeaways
What 5.2 really proves
  • Energy in atoms is fundamentally quantized: electrons can only occupy certain energy levels and make jumps of specific size.
  • Bohr’s model saves Rutherford’s nuclear atom by forbidding continuous energy loss from allowed orbits.
  • Emission/absorption spectra are direct experimental fingerprints of electron energy levels.
  • Bohr’s theory is a crucial stepping stone: excellent for H-like atoms, but too simple for multi-electron atoms → need full quantum mechanics.
5.3
Beyond Bohr — Quantum Mechanical Model
Electrons as waves, uncertainty in their motion, and orbitals as probability clouds.
R Roots — Phenomena
Why Bohr wasn’t enough
  • Bohr works well for hydrogen but fails to predict spectra for multi-electron atoms.
  • Bohr assumes quantized energy levels but cannot explain why they exist from deeper principles.
  • Experiments show electrons produce diffraction and interference patterns like waves (Davisson–Germer experiment).
  • Precision measurements reveal fine details in spectra and behaviour that require a more complete model.
T Trunk — Core Theory
de Broglie, Heisenberg, Schrödinger
  • de Broglie: particles have wave nature; λ = h/mv → wave-particle duality.
  • Standing wave condition: only orbits that fit an integer number of wavelengths are allowed → explains quantized energies.
  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle: impossible to know both exact position and exact momentum of a particle at the same time.
  • Schrödinger wave equation: describes electrons by wave functions ψ; ψ² gives probability density → orbitals.
B Branches — Sub-concepts
Wave behaviour & probability
  • For macroscopic objects (baseballs, cars), λ is so tiny it’s unobservable → classical particle behaviour is fine.
  • For electrons, λ is comparable to atomic size → wave behaviour dominates and must be included.
  • Orbitals are 3D probability clouds, not little circular paths; they show where an electron is most likely to be found.
  • Quantum mechanics replaces “where is the electron exactly?” with “what is the probability of finding it here?”.
L Leaves — Facts & Tools
Equations & principles
  • de Broglie wavelength: λ = h/mv — bigger mass → smaller λ; smaller mass → bigger λ.
  • Uncertainty: Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π (or m·Δv·Δx ≥ h/2π); more certainty in one quantity means less in the other.
  • ψ: wave function; ψ²: probability density of finding an electron in a region of space.
  • Electrons cannot be localized particles with well-defined paths around the nucleus in the classical sense.
F Fruit — Big Takeaways
What 5.3 really proves
  • At atomic scale, the sharp line between “particle” and “wave” disappears → electrons are both.
  • Quantization arises naturally from wave conditions and boundary restrictions, not from ad-hoc rules.
  • Uncertainty and probability are built into the fabric of matter; they are not just due to bad instruments.
  • The quantum mechanical model is the foundation for all later topics: orbitals, bonding, molecular shapes, spectroscopy, and more.
5.4
Applying Quantum Mechanics to Electrons in Atoms
Quantum numbers, orbital shapes, electron configurations, and how they control chemical behaviour.
R Roots — Conceptual Base
From H-atom to many-electron atoms
  • Hydrogen shows discrete energy levels with clear spectral lines → orbitals are real physical states.
  • Multi-electron atoms must obey the same quantum rules, but electron–electron repulsion alters orbital energies.
  • We need a systematic way to label orbitals and electrons → quantum numbers.
  • Observed periodic patterns (periodic table) suggest structured filling of energy levels and sublevels.
T Trunk — Core Structure
Quantum numbers & orbitals
  • Principal quantum number n: energy level and orbital size (1, 2, 3…).
  • Angular momentum quantum number ℓ: orbital shape (0 = s, 1 = p, 2 = d, 3 = f).
  • Magnetic quantum number mℓ: orientation of an orbital in space (−ℓ … +ℓ).
  • Spin quantum number ms: electron spin (+½ or −½), giving two electrons per orbital with opposite spins.
B Branches — Sub-concepts
Shapes, filling order & rules
  • s orbitals: spherical; 1 per level. p: dumbbells; 3 per level. d: 5 complex shapes. f: 7 even more complex shapes.
  • For hydrogen, all orbitals with the same n are degenerate; for multi-electron atoms, sublevels split (2s ≠ 2p, etc.).
  • Aufbau principle: electrons fill the lowest-energy orbitals available first.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers; max 2 electrons per orbital.
  • Hund’s rule: in degenerate orbitals, electrons occupy them singly with parallel spins before pairing.
  • Practical filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s… (diagonal diagram).
L Leaves — Skills & Details
Electron configurations & ions
  • Configuration notation: nℓnumber of electrons, e.g. 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶.
  • Core notation uses noble gas in brackets, e.g. [Ne] 3s² 3p³ for phosphorus.
  • Cations: remove electrons from highest-energy orbitals first (usually 4s before 3d for transition metals).
  • Anions: add electrons to the next available orbital following the filling order.
  • Isoelectronic species have the same electron configuration (e.g. O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺).
F Fruit — Big Takeaways
Why 5.4 matters for chemistry
  • The periodic table is basically a visual map of orbital filling patterns (s-block, p-block, d-block, f-block).
  • Valence electrons (outermost s and p, plus relevant d in transition metals) control bonding, ion formation, and reactivity.
  • Trends in atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity all flow from how orbitals are filled and shielded.
  • Once you can “see” electron configurations and orbitals, explaining bonding, shapes, and reactivity becomes much easier and more systematic.
