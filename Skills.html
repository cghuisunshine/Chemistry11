1.1

Staying Safe Around Matter

lab equipment ¬∑ WHMIS ¬∑ emergency skills

Safety is the first skill in chemistry. This section explains how to recognize and use laboratory equipment, interpret WHMIS and household hazard symbols, operate safety devices, and follow safe lab behaviour from set-up to clean-up.

Roots Core Ideas & Definitions
click to collapse ‚ñ∂
Big picture: the safest chemist is the one who understands both what equipment does and why procedures exist. Safety is not just rules; it is applied understanding of matter and risk.

Laboratory glassware.

  • Beakers ‚Äì wide-mouthed cylinders used to hold, mix, and roughly pour liquids. Their graduations are approximate, not for precise measurement.
  • Erlenmeyer flasks ‚Äì conical bodies with narrow necks, excellent for swirling solutions with minimal splashing and for reactions that might release gases.
  • Florence flasks ‚Äì round-bottom flasks that heat liquids evenly; their shape avoids ‚Äúhot spots.‚Äù
  • Test tubes ‚Äì small glass cylinders used for small-scale reactions and heating; easy to observe changes.
  • Evaporating dishes and crucibles ‚Äì ceramic containers that withstand high temperatures: evaporating dishes for boiling off solvent; crucibles for red-hot heating of solids.
  • Funnels ‚Äì direct liquids and support filter paper for separating solids from liquids.
  • Watch glasses ‚Äì shallow concave dishes used as covers or to hold small samples for observation or drying.

Volumetric measuring glassware.

  • Graduated cylinders ‚Äì cylinders with precise volume markings; used for reasonably accurate volume measurement.
  • Burettes ‚Äì tall, narrow tubes with a stopcock at the bottom; deliver controlled volumes, crucial in titrations.
  • Pipettes ‚Äì glass tubes that deliver fixed volumes (volumetric pipettes) or variable volumes (graduated pipettes). They require a bulb; you never mouth-pipette.
  • Thermometers ‚Äì measure temperature; the bulb must be fully immersed in the substance being measured.

Metal hardware.

  • Ring stands, ring clamps, and utility clamps ‚Äì form stable frameworks for supporting beakers, flasks, or burettes over a burner or on a bench.
  • Bunsen burners ‚Äì gas burners with adjustable air intake; more air produces a hotter, blue flame, less air a cooler, yellow flame.
  • Tongs ‚Äì specialized for beakers, crucibles, or test tubes to safely handle hot equipment.
  • Ceramic pads (wire gauze) ‚Äì sit on a ring clamp to support glassware while heating, spreading the flame‚Äôs heat.
  • Scoopulas ‚Äì mini metal scoops for transferring solid chemicals; not for stirring.

Hazard communication systems.

  • WHMIS (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System) ‚Äì Canada‚Äôs system for classifying hazardous materials, using standardized symbols and requiring training and data sheets.
  • MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheet) ‚Äì a detailed document for each chemical describing its identity, hazards, first-aid measures, storage, and handling.
  • Household hazard symbols ‚Äì simplified icons on consumer products; octagon borders indicate danger from the contents, triangle borders from the container.

Core lab safety equipment.

  • Fume hood ‚Äì ventilated enclosure that pulls toxic fumes away from the user and out of the room.
  • Eyewash station ‚Äì provides a continuous, gentle stream of water to flush eyes for 10‚Äì15 minutes after chemical exposure; contacts must be removed.
  • Safety shower ‚Äì floods the body with water when chemicals spill over a large area of skin; contaminated clothing is removed and rinsing continues for at least 10‚Äì15 minutes.
  • Fire extinguisher ‚Äì most lab extinguishers are CO‚ÇÇ and are used for class A, B, and C fires (solids, liquids, electrical). They are not used directly on people.
  • Fire blanket ‚Äì used with STOP‚ÄìDROP‚ÄìROLL to smother flames on a person‚Äôs clothing.
  • Gas shut-off ‚Äì cuts gas supply to all burners in an emergency; usually a large red handle or button.
  • Spill control and first-aid kits ‚Äì contain absorbents, neutralizers (e.g., baking soda for acids, vinegar for bases) and supplies to treat minor injuries.
  • Glass disposal box ‚Äì thick container for broken glass only; glass is never placed in regular garbage.

General safety behaviour.

  • Read the entire lab procedure before starting.
  • Prepare data tables in your notebook ahead of time.
  • Wear safety goggles and a lab apron whenever chemicals, glass, or heat are in use.
  • Tie back long hair; avoid loose clothing and dangling jewellery.
  • Wear closed-toed shoes; no sandals.
  • Never eat, drink, or chew gum in the lab.
  • Never taste chemicals and never pipette by mouth.
  • Work with a business-like attitude and keep benches tidy.

Heating techniques and patterns.

  • When heating a test tube, hold it at about a 45¬∞ angle and move the tube slowly through the flame. Always point the open end away from yourself and others.
  • Check temperature by bringing your hand near (not onto) glassware; if you can‚Äôt keep your hand nearby, it is still hot.
  • Flammable liquids are heated using a hot plate or water bath, not an open Bunsen flame.

Chemical handling rules.

  • Smelling chemicals: never stick your nose over the container. Instead, gently waft vapours towards your face with your hand if instructed.
  • Read labels carefully twice before using any reagent: once when you pick it up, and again before you pour.
  • Hold reagent bottles with the label toward the palm so drips run down the back, protecting the label.
  • Never return unused chemicals to the stock bottle; this prevents contamination. Instead, follow your teacher‚Äôs disposal instructions.
  • Lubricate glass tubing with water or glycerin before inserting it into a rubber stopper and hold it close to the end being inserted to reduce the risk of breakage and cuts.

Critical rule: acid and water.

Always add acid to water, never water to acid. When you pour water into concentrated acid, the first drops can boil instantly and splash acid. Adding acid slowly into a larger volume of water lets heat spread out safely.

Hazard systems in practice.

  • WHMIS symbols tell you what type of danger a chemical poses: flammable, corrosive, toxic, biohazardous, oxidizing, compressed gas, and so on.
  • The MSDS explains exactly what to do if the chemical is swallowed, inhaled, spilled, or splashed on skin or eyes; it also gives storage, reactivity, and disposal guidance.
  • Household symbols on cleaners, fuels, and pesticides warn about everyday hazards. The border shape quickly tells you whether the contents or the container is dangerous.

Emergency response pattern.

  • Chemical in eyes: go immediately to the eyewash station; rinse while holding eyelids open for at least 10‚Äì15 minutes and tell your teacher.
  • Chemical on skin: flush with lots of water; if a large spill, use the safety shower and remove contaminated clothing.
  • Fire on clothing: STOP‚ÄìDROP‚ÄìROLL while someone covers you with a fire blanket.
  • Fire in equipment: turn off gas or power if safe, then use an appropriate extinguisher; never spray a person with a fire extinguisher.
  • Serious spill: evacuate area if necessary, alert the teacher, and use spill kits under direction; neutralize acids with sodium bicarbonate and bases with dilute acetic acid (vinegar) when told to do so.

End-of-lab routine.

  • Turn off burners, gas, and electrical equipment.
  • Dispose of chemicals in designated containers; never down the sink unless instructed.
  • Put broken glass into the glass disposal box.
  • Wash and rinse glassware; allow it to air-dry.
  • Wipe benches and wash your hands before leaving.
  • Label any setups that must remain overnight clearly with your name and the contents.

Example: choosing the right glassware.

  • To measure exactly 25.00 mL of a solution, choose a volumetric pipette or burette, not a beaker or Erlenmeyer flask.
  • To heat a solid to red hot, use a crucible on a clay triangle, not a beaker.
  • To heat a solution gently while stirring, use a beaker on a ceramic pad over a Bunsen burner or a hot plate.

Example: using an MSDS.

Suppose you are given a bottle of concentrated hydrochloric acid. From the MSDS you learn that it is corrosive to skin and eyes, produces choking fumes, and reacts with metals to release hydrogen gas. This tells you to:

  • Work in a fume hood for large volumes or strong fumes.
  • Wear goggles, gloves, and an apron.
  • Keep away from reactive metals and open flames (because of hydrogen).
  • Flush skin or eyes with water for 15 minutes if exposed.

Common pitfalls.

  • Reading the meniscus from above or below eye level results in parallax error; always read at eye level.
  • Heating a closed container can cause a dangerous pressure build-up and possible explosion.
  • Pipetting by mouth is extremely unsafe and forbidden; use a pipette bulb or pump.
  • Returning unused reagent to the stock bottle can contaminate the entire bottle; discard excess as instructed instead.

Mental checklist before starting any lab.

  • Do I know where the eyewash, shower, and extinguisher are?
  • Have I read the entire procedure and asked questions about anything unclear?
  • Is my bench clear of food, drinks, and clutter?
  • Do I have goggles, apron, and proper shoes on?

Skill goal: by the end of this section, students should treat lab safety procedures as natural habits, not just rules memorized for a test.

Laboratory & Reporting Skills

This section explains how to record observations, use the scientific method, write and manipulate scientific notation, and represent relationships with graphs and equations.

Observations.

  • Qualitative observations describe qualities‚Äîcolour, odour, texture, state of matter, and so on‚Äîwithout numbers. Example: ‚ÄúThe precipitate is bright yellow.‚Äù
  • Quantitative observations include numbers and units, such as mass, volume, temperature, length, and time. Example: ‚ÄúThe solution‚Äôs temperature increased from 20.0 ¬∞C to 27.5 ¬∞C.‚Äù
  • Physical properties can be observed without changing the substance (colour, density, melting point).
  • Chemical properties are observed when a substance undergoes a chemical change (reacts with acid, rusts in air, burns).

The scientific method.

  • Observation: careful noticing and recording of phenomena; can be qualitative or quantitative.
  • Hypothesis: a testable explanation or prediction often written in ‚Äúif‚Ä¶then‚Ä¶‚Äù form, linking cause and effect.
  • Experiment: a controlled test where variables are manipulated and data are gathered to support or refute the hypothesis.
  • Theory: a well-supported explanation that unifies a broad set of observations and experiments (explains why).
  • Law: a statement describing a consistently observed pattern in nature (describes what happens, not why).

Scientific notation.

  • A number is written as A √ó 10‚Åø , where A (the mantissa) is between 1 and 10 and n (the exponent) is an integer.
  • Positive exponents represent large numbers (move the decimal to the right); negative exponents represent small numbers (move decimal left).
  • Example: 0.000 312 = 3.12 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥; 58 200 = 5.82 √ó 10‚Å¥.

Variables in experiments.

  • Independent variable ‚Äì the quantity you choose or control (e.g., time, mass added, temperature setting) and place on the x-axis.
  • Dependent variable ‚Äì the quantity that responds and depends on the independent variable (e.g., extension of a spring, reaction yield), placed on the y-axis.

Graphing and relationships.

  • A linear (direct) relationship has the form y = kx + b , where k is the slope and b the y-intercept.
  • If b = 0, the relationship is directly proportional: y = kx .
  • Inverse relationships have the form y = k/x .

Measuring & Recording Significant Data

SI system ¬∑ uncertainty ¬∑ significant figures

This section explains SI units, accuracy vs. precision, types of error, how to record uncertainty, and how to use significant figures correctly in measurements and calculations.

Roots Core Ideas & Definitions
click to collapse ‚ñ∂

The SI system.

  • The International System of Units (SI) is the globally agreed-upon system used in science and engineering.
  • Key base units: metre (m) for length, kilogram (kg) for mass, second (s) for time, kelvin (K) for temperature, mole (mol) for amount of substance, ampere (A) for electric current, candela (cd) for luminous intensity.
  • Other units (e.g. m/s, J, Pa, g/mL) are derived from combinations of these base units.

Accuracy and precision.

  • Accuracy describes how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value.
  • Precision (reproducibility) describes how close repeated measurements are to each other; it also reflects the fineness of the instrument‚Äôs scale.
  • You can have high precision but low accuracy if an instrument is consistently mis-calibrated (systematic error).

Types of error.

  • Systematic error ‚Äì consistently shifts all values in one direction; caused by faulty equipment or biased technique (e.g. balance that reads 0.05 g too high).
  • Random error ‚Äì leads to scatter in data; caused by tiny fluctuations and limits in reading instruments (e.g. timing with a hand stopwatch).

Uncertainty in measurements.

  • Every measurement has uncertainty; we report this explicitly: measured value ¬± uncertainty.
  • Uncertainty is based on the instrument‚Äôs smallest scale division, usually between ¬±0.1 and ¬±0.5 of that division.
  • Example: a 50 mL cylinder marked every 1 mL might give a volume as 23.4 mL ¬± 0.1 mL.

Significant figures (sig figs).

  • Significant figures are the digits in a measurement that are known reliably, plus one final estimated digit.
  • They indicate how much information (certainty) a measurement truly contains.

SI writing rules.

  • Put a space between the number and unit: 4.6 g, 22 ¬∞C.
  • Do not pluralize unit symbols (5 kg, not 5 kgs).
  • Use lowercase for unit names, except ‚ÄúCelsius.‚Äù
  • Write a leading zero before decimals less than 1 (0.54 g).
  • Group long numbers with spaces (12 500 000), not commas.
  • Avoid non-SI abbreviations such as ‚Äúsec‚Äù or ‚Äúcc.‚Äù

Combining uncertainties.

  • For addition or subtraction, add the absolute uncertainties: if V‚ÇÅ = 27.6 ¬± 0.2 mL and V‚ÇÇ = 14.8 ¬± 0.2 mL, then V‚ÇÅ + V‚ÇÇ = 42.4 ¬± 0.4 mL.
  • Absolute uncertainty is the difference between your measured value and the accepted value: Œî = measured ‚àí accepted .
  • Percentage error expresses error relative to the accepted value: percent error = (measured ‚àí accepted)/accepted √ó 100% .

Counting significant figures.

  • All non-zero digits are significant (246 ‚Üí 3 sig figs).
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant (2094 ‚Üí 4 sig figs).
  • Leading zeros are not significant (0.00750 ‚Üí 3 sig figs: 7, 5, 0).
  • Trailing zeros are significant if the number has a decimal (5700. ‚Üí 4 sig figs; 5700 ‚Üí 2 sig figs unless specified).
  • Scientific notation is helpful: 2.50 √ó 10¬≥ clearly has 3 sig figs.

Sig fig rules in calculations.

  • Multiplication & division: result has the same number of sig figs as the value with the fewest sig figs.
  • Addition & subtraction: result is rounded to the least precise decimal place among the inputs (not the fewest sig figs).
  • Exact counts (e.g. 12 eggs, 3 beakers) and defined conversions (1 cm = 10 mm) do not limit sig figs.

Analysis of Units & Conversions

dimensional analysis ¬∑ derived units ¬∑ temperature

This section shows how to convert between units using dimensional analysis, handle derived units and exponents, and move between Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin temperature scales.

Roots Core Ideas & Definitions
click to collapse ‚ñ∂

Dimensional analysis.

  • Dimensional analysis (factor-label method) is a systematic way to convert units by multiplying by fractions (conversion factors) equal to 1.
  • A conversion factor is built from an equality such as 1 inch = 2.54 cm, which can be written as: 1 in / 2.54 cm or 2.54 cm / 1 in .
  • Units cancel like algebraic symbols; the final unit should be the one you are solving for.

Derived units.

  • Units like m/s, km/h, g/mL, and Pa (N/m¬≤) are derived units because they combine more than one base unit.
  • When converting derived units, you must convert the units in both the numerator and the denominator.

Metric prefixes.

  • Common prefixes: kilo- (10¬≥), centi- (10‚Åª¬≤), milli- (10‚Åª¬≥), micro- (10‚Åª‚Å∂), nano- (10‚Åª‚Åπ), mega- (10‚Å∂), giga- (10‚Åπ).
  • Converting between prefixed units is simply shifting decimal places because the system is based on powers of ten.

Temperature scales.

  • Celsius (¬∞C) ‚Äì water freezes at 0 ¬∞C and boils at 100 ¬∞C at standard pressure.
  • Fahrenheit (¬∞F) ‚Äì water freezes at 32 ¬∞F and boils at 212 ¬∞F; used commonly in the United States.
  • Kelvin (K) ‚Äì absolute temperature scale; 0 K is absolute zero, where particles have minimal thermal motion.

General dimensional analysis procedure.

  1. Write down the given value as a fraction (over 1).
  2. Multiply by conversion factors so that unwanted units cancel and the desired unit remains.
  3. Check that all units except the final one have cancelled before calculating.
  4. Perform the multiplication and division, then round using sig figs.

Example ‚Äì yards to centimetres.

1.00 yd √ó 3 ft/1 yd √ó 12 in/1 ft √ó 2.54 cm/1 in

Units cancel: yd ‚Üí ft ‚Üí in ‚Üí cm. Numerical answer: 91.44 cm ‚Üí 91.4 cm (3 sig figs).

Result ‚âà 15.3 m/s (3 sig figs).

  • If 1 m = 100 cm, then 1 m¬≤ = (100 cm)¬≤ = 10‚Å¥ cm¬≤ and 1 m¬≥ = (100 cm)¬≥ = 10‚Å∂ cm¬≥.
  • When converting squared or cubed units, the conversion factor itself must be squared or cubed.

  • A density like 7.13 g/mL can be used to convert between mass and volume: 7.13 g/1 mL or 1 mL/7.13 g .
