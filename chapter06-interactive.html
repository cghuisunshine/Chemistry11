Big Idea Periodic table = predictive map tap to open ▾
The causal chain (the “why” of the chapter)
double-click to copy 
electron configuration → periodic trends → bonding → shape → polarity → IMF → observable properties

If you can explain each arrow in that chain, you can explain almost any Chem 11 periodic-trends question.

What “periodic” means
  • Periodic = properties repeat in a regular pattern.
  • Groups (columns) share similar chemistry because they have similar valence electrons.
  • Periods (rows) relate to number of occupied energy levels (principal energy levels).
Exam mindset Don’t memorize trends blindly. Always justify using: shielding Zeff distance electron repulsion
6.1 Development of the Periodic Table tap to open ▾
Key historical milestones
  • Dobereiner (Triads): sets of 3 elements with similar properties.
  • Newlands (Octaves): properties repeat every 8 elements (early pattern idea).
  • Mendeleev: organized by mass + behavior, left gaps, predicted unknown elements.
  • Moseley: modern table organized by atomic number (Z) (protons).
Why atomic number is the “correct” key

Atomic number determines electron count (for neutral atoms), which determines electron configuration and therefore chemical behavior.

double-click to copy 
Modern Periodic Law:
When elements are arranged by increasing atomic number,
their properties recur periodically.
Core idea Similar chemistry in a group is mainly explained by same valence-electron pattern.
6.2 Periodic Trends (Size, IE, EN) tap to open ▾
Two forces that explain almost every trend
Factor What it is Effect
Shielding Inner electrons block nuclear pull on valence electrons More shielding → outer electrons held less tightly
Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) Net pull felt by valence electrons Higher Zeff → smaller radius, higher IE, higher EN
double-click to copy 
Across a period: Zeff ↑ (shielding ~ constant) → radius ↓, IE ↑, EN ↑
Down a group: n ↑ + shielding ↑ → radius ↑, IE ↓, EN ↓
Trend Atomic Radius (size) tap ▾
Trend directions
  • Down a group: radius increases (more energy levels, bigger electron cloud).
  • Across a period: radius decreases (Zeff increases, pulls electrons inward).
Reasoning sentence “Across a period, protons increase but shielding is similar, so Zeff increases and electrons are pulled closer.”
Ion size rules
  • Cations are smaller than parent atoms (loss of e⁻ → less repulsion, tighter hold).
  • Anions are larger than parent atoms (gain e⁻ → more repulsion, expanded cloud).
  • Isoelectronic series: same electrons → more protons = smaller ion.
Trend Ionization Energy (IE) tap ▾
Definition
double-click to copy 
Ionization Energy (IE):
Energy required to remove one electron from a gaseous atom.

High IE means the atom holds its electrons tightly. Low IE means it loses electrons easily (metal behavior).

Trend directions
  • Down a group: IE decreases (outer e⁻ farther, more shielding).
  • Across a period: IE increases (Zeff increases).
Common exceptions Small “dips” happen due to subshell structure and pairing repulsion (e.g., oxygen vs nitrogen).
Trend Electronegativity (EN) tap ▾
Definition
double-click to copy 
Electronegativity (EN):
Ability of a bonded atom to attract shared electrons.

EN is about bonding (shared electrons), not isolated atoms.

Trend directions
  • Across a period: EN increases (Zeff ↑, radius ↓).
  • Down a group: EN decreases (bonding electrons farther away).
  • Highest: F (fluorine).
Interpretation Larger EN difference → bond is more polar → more ionic character.
ΔEN (rough guideline) Bond type What it means
≈ 0 Non-polar covalent Equal sharing
0.4 – 1.7 Polar covalent Unequal sharing
> 1.7 Ionic (mostly) Electron transfer dominates
6.3 Bonding Types (Ionic / Covalent / Metallic) tap to open ▾
At-a-glance bonding map
Elements involved Bond type Main idea
Metal + non-metal Ionic Electron transfer → ions attract in a lattice
Non-metal + non-metal Covalent Shared electron pairs between nuclei
Metal + metal Metallic “Sea of electrons” around positive metal ions
Ionic bonding: what to say on tests
  • Large ΔEN → electron transfer likely.
  • Forms cations + anions → electrostatic attraction.
  • Solid structure = crystal lattice.
  • Formula is simplest whole-number ratio (formula unit).
Common pitfall Ionic compounds do not form “molecules” in the solid state; they form a lattice.
Covalent bonding: polarity + bond order
  • Shared electrons; bond is between nuclei.
  • ΔEN controls polarity (dipole).
  • Single / double / triple bonds: more shared pairs → shorter + stronger bonds.

Bond order ↑ → bond length ↓ and bond energy ↑
6.4 Lewis Structures (Rules + Resonance) tap to open ▾
Lewis structure algorithm (copy-ready)
double-click to copy 
Lewis Steps:
1) Count total valence e− (add for negative charge, subtract for positive)
2) Choose skeleton (least EN usually central; H never central)
3) Connect atoms with single bonds (2 e− each)
4) Complete octets for outer atoms
5) Put leftover e− on central atom
6) If central lacks octet, create multiple bonds
7) Check total e− count + formal charges
Octet rule exceptions
  • Incomplete octet: Be (4), B/Al (6) common
  • Expanded octet: period 3+ atoms (can exceed 8)
  • Odd-electron: radicals (rare in Chem 11)
Teacher line “Lewis structures track electrons; VSEPR tracks electron groups.”
Resonance (delocalization)

Resonance means one Lewis structure is not enough. The real structure is a hybrid where electrons are spread out (delocalized).

double-click to copy 
Resonance rules:
• Only electrons move (π bonds/lone pairs), not atoms
• Resonance structures must keep the same total valence electrons
• The real molecule is NOT “switching”; it’s an average (hybrid)
6.5 VSEPR, Shape, and Molecular Polarity tap to open ▾
VSEPR core rule
double-click to copy 
VSEPR:
Electron groups repel and arrange to minimize repulsion.
(Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs.)

Count electron groups around the central atom: each bond (single/double/triple) counts as 1 group; each lone pair counts as 1 group.

Common VSEPR shapes (Chem 11 essential set)
Electron Groups Typical Formula Shape Example
2 AX₂ Linear (180°) CO₂
3 AX₃ / AX₂E Trigonal planar / Bent BF₃ / SO₂
4 AX₄ / AX₃E / AX₂E₂ Tetrahedral / Trig pyramidal / Bent CH₄ / NH₃ / H₂O
5 AX₅ Trigonal bipyramidal PCl₅
6 AX₆ Octahedral SF₆
Bond polarity vs molecular polarity
  • Bond polarity depends on ΔEN (dipole on a bond).
  • Molecular polarity depends on both bond polarity AND shape.
  • If dipoles cancel (symmetry) → molecule can be non-polar.
Fast polarity checklist
double-click to copy 
Polarity checklist:
1) Are there polar bonds? (ΔEN > ~0.4)
2) Is the geometry symmetric?
→ If not symmetric, dipoles don’t cancel → polar molecule
Example CO₂ has polar C=O bonds, but it’s linear and symmetric → non-polar overall.
IMF Intermolecular Forces (Dispersion, Dipole, H-bond, Ion-dipole) tap to open ▾
IMF strength ladder (copy-ready)
double-click to copy 
IMF strength (typical):
Dispersion < Dipole–dipole < Hydrogen bonding < Ion–dipole

Stronger IMFs usually mean higher boiling point, higher melting point, higher viscosity, and lower volatility.

IMF table
Force Applies to What causes it Common effect
Dispersion (London) All particles Temporary induced dipoles Increases with molar mass / surface area
Dipole–dipole Polar molecules Permanent dipoles align Raises bp compared to similar non-polar
Hydrogen bonding H–N, H–O, H–F Strong dipole–dipole with H attached to N/O/F High bp (water), strong cohesion
Ion–dipole Ions + polar solvent Ion attracts dipole end of molecule Explains dissolving salts in water
Solubility rule “Like dissolves like”: polar with polar (or ionic), non-polar with non-polar.
Practice Mini Quiz (click to reveal answers) tap to open ▾

Q1. Why does atomic radius decrease across a period?

Write one sentence using shielding and effective nuclear charge (Zeff).

Across a period, proton number increases while shielding changes little, so Zeff increases and pulls valence electrons closer, decreasing radius.

Q2. Which is larger: Na or Na⁺? Why?

Na is larger. Na⁺ has lost its outer valence electron shell and has reduced electron–electron repulsion; remaining electrons feel stronger attraction to the nucleus.

Q3. Decide bond type by ΔEN: ΔEN = 1.2

Is it non-polar covalent, polar covalent, or ionic?

Polar covalent. A ΔEN around 0.4–1.7 usually indicates unequal sharing (polar covalent).

Q4. Use VSEPR: What is the shape of NH₃?

Count electron groups and give the molecular shape name.

NH₃ has 4 electron groups around N (3 bonds + 1 lone pair). Electron geometry is tetrahedral; molecular shape is trigonal pyramidal.

Q5. Which has higher boiling point: CH₄ or CH₃OH? Why?

CH₃OH. It can hydrogen bond (O–H), which is much stronger than the dispersion forces in non-polar CH₄.
