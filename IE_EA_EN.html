Ionization Energy, Electron Affinity, Electronegativity

Master Summary

This page explains the three key atomic properties:

  • Ionization Energy (IE)
  • Electron Affinity (EA)
  • Electronegativity (EN)

It also shows how they are related and how electronegativity can be computed from bond energies or from ionization energy and electron affinity.

1

Ionization Energy (IE)

Definition

Ionization energy is the energy required to remove one electron from a gaseous atom:

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻

Meaning (simple)

Ionization energy tells us how strongly an atom holds onto its own electrons:

  • High IE → it is hard to remove an electron (strong attraction to the nucleus).
  • Low IE → it is easy to remove an electron (weaker attraction; typical for metals).

Periodic Trend

  • Across a period (left → right): IE increases
    Nuclear charge increases, so electrons are pulled closer and are harder to remove.
  • Down a group (top → bottom): IE decreases
    More shells and more shielding, so the outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and easier to remove.

Quantum Reasons

Ionization energy depends on several quantum factors:

  • Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) – higher Zeff → higher IE.
  • Principal quantum number (n) – larger n → electron farther from nucleus → lower IE.
  • Subshell energy – s < p < d < f in energy; electrons in higher-energy subshells are slightly easier to remove.
  • Electron–electron repulsion – explains anomalies (e.g., Be vs. B, N vs. O) where removing an electron can relieve repulsion.
2

Electron Affinity (EA)

Definition

Electron affinity is the energy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron:

X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g)

Often, electron affinity is given as a negative value when energy is released.

Meaning (simple)

Electron affinity tells us how much an atom wants an extra electron:

  • More negative EA → the atom strongly releases energy when it gains an electron (favorable process; strong “desire”).
  • EA ≈ 0 or positive → the atom does not particularly want an extra electron or it is energetically unfavorable.

Periodic Trend

  • Across a period: EA becomes more negative
    Atoms get closer to a full valence shell, so gaining an electron is more favorable.
  • Down a group: EA becomes less negative
    New electrons are added farther from the nucleus and experience more shielding, so the attraction is weaker.

Important Exceptions

  • Noble gases (Group 18): EA ≈ 0
    They already have a full valence shell; adding another electron is not favorable.
  • Group 2 (Be, Mg, etc.): EA is small or near 0
    They have filled s subshells; adding an electron would go into a higher-energy p subshell.
  • Nitrogen (p³ configuration): EA is less negative than expected
    Adding an electron breaks the symmetric half-filled p³ configuration, increasing repulsion.
3

Electronegativity (EN)

Definition

Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract electrons in a chemical bond.

It is a dimensionless quantity (no units) and is not measured directly but inferred from other properties (like bond energies, IE, and EA).

Meaning (simple)

  • High EN → the atom strongly pulls shared electrons in a covalent bond toward itself (e.g., F, O, N, Cl).
  • Low EN → the atom weakly pulls shared electrons (typical of metals; they tend to lose electrons instead).

Most electronegative element: Fluorine, with a Pauling electronegativity of about 3.98.

Periodic Trend

  • Across a period: EN increases
    Higher effective nuclear charge and smaller atomic radius make the nucleus better at attracting bonding electrons.
  • Down a group: EN decreases
    Atomic radius increases and shielding increases, so the nucleus is less effective at pulling bonding electrons.
4

How Ionization Energy, Electron Affinity, and Electronegativity Are Related

All three quantities reflect the same underlying idea: the attraction between the positive nucleus and electrons. They are different viewpoints on how strongly an atom interacts with electrons.

Property What it asks What it measures Type of electron
Ionization Energy (IE) How hard is it to remove an electron? Attraction to the atom's own electrons Internal electron
Electron Affinity (EA) How much energy is released when gaining an electron? Attraction to an incoming electron New incoming electron
Electronegativity (EN) How strongly does the atom pull shared electrons? Attraction to shared electrons in a bond Shared bonding electron

Key Relationship

Atoms with both:

  • High ionization energy (they do not want to lose electrons), and
  • Highly negative electron affinity (they strongly want to gain electrons)

will typically have high electronegativity, because they strongly attract electrons in any context, including shared electrons in a bond.

High IE + Highly negative EA ⇒ High EN

Examples

  • Nonmetals like F, O, N, Cl
    They have high IE and highly negative EA → they strongly attract bonding electrons → very high EN.
  • Metals like Na, K
    They have low IE and EA values close to zero → they prefer to lose electrons and form cations → low EN.
Metals vs. Nonmetals:
Metals have low IE and small or slightly negative EA, so they usually form cations (lose electrons) rather than pulling electrons toward themselves. Nonmetals have high IE and more negative EA, so they attract electrons strongly and often form anions or strongly polar covalent bonds.
5

How Electronegativity Is Computed

Electronegativity is not measured directly. Instead, chemists use models and formulas to assign electronegativity values from measurable quantities such as bond energies, ionization energies, and electron affinities.

A. Pauling Electronegativity (Most Common)

Linus Pauling used bond energies to define electronegativity. He compared the energy of an A–B bond to the average of A–A and B–B bonds.

|χA − χB| = √( DAB − (DAA + DBB) / 2 )

Here, D represents bond dissociation energies (in consistent units like eV or kJ/mol).

If the A–B bond is more stable (stronger) than expected from the average, Pauling attributed that extra stability to a difference in electronegativity between A and B.

He then chose fluorine as the reference and set χ(F) ≈ 3.98, calculating the rest relative to that.

Important: The electronegativity values you see in most periodic tables in school (F = 3.98, O ≈ 3.44, N ≈ 3.04, etc.) are usually on the Pauling scale.

B. Mulliken Electronegativity

Mulliken suggested a very intuitive formula, using both ionization energy and electron affinity:

χ = (IE + EA) / 2

IE and EA must be in the same units, typically electron-volts (eV) or kJ/mol (with appropriate conversion).

Interpretation:

  • High IE → the atom strongly resists losing electrons (it holds them tightly).
  • High (very negative) EA → the atom strongly wants to gain electrons.

The average of these two quantities gives a measure of how strongly the atom attracts electrons in general, including in bonds. This matches our physical intuition very well.

When either IE increases or EA becomes more negative, Mulliken electronegativity increases. This aligns with the idea that EN is essentially a combination of “how strongly I hold electrons” and “how strongly I want more electrons.”

C. Allred–Rochow Electronegativity

The Allred–Rochow scale connects electronegativity directly to the Coulombic attraction between the nucleus and a valence electron:

χ = Zeff / r²

where:

  • Zeff is the effective nuclear charge felt by the valence electrons.
  • r is the covalent radius (distance from the nucleus to the valence shell).

Because Coulombic force is proportional to Z / r², this formula captures the idea that electronegativity is stronger when:

  • The effective nuclear charge is higher, and/or
  • The valence electrons are closer to the nucleus (smaller radius).
6

Big Picture Summary

All three quantities describe how an atom interacts with electrons, just in different situations:

Ionization Energy → holding electrons Electron Affinity → wanting electrons Electronegativity → pulling shared electrons

Core idea:

Electronegativity is essentially the combined effect of ionization energy and electron affinity.

If an atom:

  • Holds its electrons very tightly (high IE), and
  • Strongly releases energy when gaining an electron (highly negative EA),

then it will also strongly attract electrons in a bond, giving it a high electronegativity.

Period Trends Recap

  • Across a period (left → right): Effective nuclear charge increases, atomic radius decreases → typically:
    • IE increases
    • EA becomes more negative
    • EN increases
  • Down a group (top → bottom): Atomic radius increases, shielding increases → typically:
    • IE decreases
    • EA becomes less negative
    • EN decreases

These three properties together explain:

  • Why metals tend to lose electrons and form cations.
  • Why nonmetals tend to gain electrons or form polar covalent bonds.
  • Why some bonds are ionic, some are polar covalent, and some are nonpolar covalent, based on electronegativity differences.
