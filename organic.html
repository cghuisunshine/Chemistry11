8.1 Simple Hydrocarbons and Isomerism

Carbon, Organic Compounds, and Hydrocarbons

üå± Roots ‚Äî What makes a compound ‚Äúorganic‚Äù? click to expand

Organic compounds are molecules that contain carbon atoms, usually bonded to other carbons and to hydrogen. They may also include elements such as nitrogen, oxygen, sulphur, phosphorus, or halogens.

Not every carbon-containing compound is considered organic. Important exceptions include:

  • Carbonates (e.g. Na‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ)
  • Carbides
  • Oxides of carbon (CO, CO‚ÇÇ)

A special subset of organic compounds are hydrocarbons ‚Äì compounds made only of carbon and hydrogen.

Carbon has four valence electrons, so each carbon atom can form four covalent bonds. This allows carbon to create:

  • Long chains (straight or branched)
  • Rings and cage-like structures
  • Single, double, and triple bonds

This bonding flexibility explains why there are millions of organic compounds.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Structural, condensed, and skeleton formulas click to expand

Organic molecules can be represented in several ways:

  • Structural formula ‚Äì shows all atoms and bonds explicitly.
  • Condensed structural formula ‚Äì groups atoms by carbon, e.g. CH‚ÇÉ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÉ.
  • Skeleton (line) formula ‚Äì only lines for bonds; carbon atoms are implied at line ends and bends, and H on C is omitted.

Example: butane (C‚ÇÑH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄ) can be written as:

  • Structural: shows each C with the correct number of H.
  • Condensed: CH‚ÇÉ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÉ
  • Skeleton: a zig-zag chain of four line segments.

In any structural form, each carbon must have four bonds (to H or other atoms).

üå± Roots ‚Äî Families of hydrocarbons click to expand

Hydrocarbons are grouped into five main families:

  • Alkanes ‚Äì only single bonds; saturated.
  • Cycloalkanes ‚Äì single bonds in a ring; saturated.
  • Alkenes ‚Äì one or more C=C double bonds; unsaturated.
  • Alkynes ‚Äì one or more C‚â°C triple bonds; unsaturated.
  • Aromatic hydrocarbons ‚Äì contain benzene rings with delocalized electrons.
üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Alkanes: formulas, prefixes, and naming rules click to expand

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with only single bonds. General formula:

CnH2n+2

Straight-chain alkanes use Greek prefixes for the number of carbons:

  • 1: meth- (methane)
  • 2: eth- (ethane)
  • 3: prop- (propane)
  • 4: but- (butane)
  • 5‚Äì10: pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-, non-, dec-

Naming branched alkanes:

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain ‚Üí this is the parent (e.g. hexane).
  2. Number the chain from the end closest to the first branch.
  3. Name branches as alkyl groups (methyl, ethyl, propyl...). Use di-, tri-, tetra- for repeated identical branches.
  4. Write the name: addresses (numbers) + branch names (alphabetical) + parent name. Numbers are separated by commas; numbers and words by hyphens.

Important detail: when alphabetizing branches, ignore di-/tri- and use the base name (e.g. ‚Äúethyl‚Äù vs ‚Äúmethyl‚Äù).

Example 1 ‚Äì Naming a branched alkane

Suppose the longest chain has 6 carbons (hexane), and there is an ethyl branch on carbon 3, and methyl branches on carbons 2 and 3. The correct name is:

3-ethyl-2,3-dimethylhexane

The numbers tell you exactly where the branches attach.

Example 2 ‚Äì Structural isomers of C‚ÇÖH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ

  • pentane (straight chain of 5 C)
  • 2-methylbutane (4 in a row + one CH‚ÇÉ branch)
  • 2,2-dimethylpropane (3 in a row + two CH‚ÇÉ branches on the middle C)

All three have the same molecular formula C‚ÇÖH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ, but different connectivity. That difference is what makes them structural isomers.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Cycloalkanes, alkenes, alkynes, and aromatic benzene click to expand

Cycloalkanes

Formed when carbon atoms make a ring with only single bonds. General formula:

CnH2n

Naming is similar to alkanes, but with the prefix cyclo-.

  • Ring with more carbons is usually the parent chain.
  • Number carbons around the ring to give branches the lowest possible set of numbers.
  • If the ring is a side group, call it a cycloalkyl branch (e.g. cyclobutyl).

Alkenes

Alkenes are hydrocarbons with at least one C=C double bond and are unsaturated, meaning more atoms can be added at that site.

General formula (for one double bond, acyclic):

CnH2n

The parent chain must include the double bond. Name ends with -ene, and the double-bond position is given the lowest possible number.

Because rotation around a double bond is restricted, alkenes can form geometric (cis/trans) isomers:

  • cis-: key groups on the same side of the double bond.
  • trans-: key groups on opposite sides.

Alkynes

Alkynes contain at least one C‚â°C triple bond, are unsaturated, and have the general formula:

CnH2n‚àí2

The parent chain must include the triple bond; name ends with -yne, and the triple bond is given the lowest possible number.

Aromatic hydrocarbons (benzene)

Benzene is a six-carbon ring with alternating single and double bonds in the Lewis structures, but in reality the electrons are delocalized around the ring.

This is shown using resonance structures or as a hexagon with a circle inside. Substituted benzenes are named by treating the ring like a parent chain or sometimes like a branch (phenyl).

For disubstituted benzenes:

  • 1,2- = ortho- (o-)
  • 1,3- = meta- (m-)
  • 1,4- = para- (p-)
üçÉ Leaves ‚Äî Quick tips & pitfalls for 8.1 click to expand
  • Always choose the longest possible carbon chain as the parent, even if it bends.
  • Number from the side that hits a branch (or double/triple bond) earliest.
  • Alkanes and cycloalkanes are saturated; alkenes and alkynes are unsaturated.
  • Cycloalkanes and alkenes can share the same formula (C‚ÇôH‚ÇÇ‚Çô), so you must look at the structure.
  • Geometric isomerism (cis/trans) only makes sense when a double bond or ring stops free rotation.

8.2 Functional Groups

Patterns That Control Reactivity

üå± Roots ‚Äî What is a functional group? click to expand

A functional group is a specific atom, group of atoms, or pattern of bonds in an organic molecule that largely determines how that molecule reacts.

Chemists often use R to represent the ‚Äúrest‚Äù of the molecule ‚Äì a hydrocarbon fragment attached to the functional group. If there are two different fragments, they can be labeled R and R‚Ä≤.

Different molecules that share the same functional group (for example, different alcohols) tend to react in similar ways, even though the rest of the structure may differ.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Catalogue of functional groups (structures & meaning) click to expand

Below are the key functional groups from this chapter, with what they look like and what they do.

Alkyl halides ‚Äì R‚ÄìX

  • X = F, Cl, Br, I (a halogen).
  • Named like branched alkanes, with halogen prefixes: fluoro-, chloro-, bromo-, iodo-.
  • Example: 2-bromo-4-chloropentane.

Alcohols ‚Äì R‚ÄìOH

  • Contain a hydroxyl (‚ÄìOH) group attached to carbon (not to a metal).
  • Different from hydroxide ion (OH‚Åª) in ionic bases.
  • Naming rules:
    • Parent chain includes the carbon with ‚ÄìOH.
    • Number the chain so ‚ÄìOH gets the lowest possible number.
    • Replace the alkane ‚Äú-e‚Äù ending with -ol (e.g. hexane ‚Üí hexan-1-ol).
  • Examples: ethanol, 3-methyl-1-butanol, 2-propanol (isopropanol).

Ethers ‚Äì R‚ÄìO‚ÄìR‚Ä≤

  • Two hydrocarbon fragments connected by an oxygen atom.
  • Common in solvents and older anesthetics (e.g., diethyl ether).
  • In this course, you mainly need to recognize this pattern, not name it systematically.

Aldehydes ‚Äì R‚ÄìCHO

  • Contain a carbonyl group (C=O) at the end of a carbon chain.
  • General pattern: R‚ÄìC(=O)‚ÄìH.
  • Examples: methanal (formaldehyde), benzaldehyde.

Ketones ‚Äì R‚ÄìCO‚ÄìR‚Ä≤

  • Also contain a carbonyl group, but it is in the middle of the chain (between two carbons).
  • General pattern: R‚ÄìC(=O)‚ÄìR‚Ä≤.
  • Example: propanone (acetone), 2-butanone.

Carboxylic acids ‚Äì R‚ÄìCOOH

  • Contain a carboxyl group: one carbonyl plus one hydroxyl on the same carbon.
  • General pattern: R‚ÄìC(=O)‚ÄìOH.
  • Weak acids often used as food preservatives (e.g. ethanoic acid in vinegar).

Esters ‚Äì R‚ÄìCOO‚ÄìR‚Ä≤

  • Look like an acid and an alcohol ‚Äústitched together‚Äù: R‚ÄìC(=O)‚ÄìO‚ÄìR‚Ä≤.
  • Commonly have fruity or pleasant odors; used in flavorings and fragrances.
  • Formed when a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol (see esterification in 8.3).

Amines ‚Äì R‚ÄìNH‚ÇÇ, R‚ÇÇNH, or R‚ÇÉN

  • Organic compounds containing nitrogen attached to carbon.
  • Often weak bases; used in dyes and pharmaceuticals.

Amides ‚Äì R‚ÄìCONH‚ÇÇ (or substituted)

  • Contain nitrogen attached to a carbonyl carbon.
  • Important in plastics (nylon) and in biology: peptide bonds between amino acids are amide bonds.
üçÉ Leaves ‚Äî Comparisons, examples, and recognition tips click to expand

Carbonyl-containing groups

  • Aldehydes and ketones each have C=O but differ in position (end vs middle).
  • Carboxylic acids and esters both contain C=O and a single-bonded O, but acids have ‚ÄìOH while esters have ‚ÄìO‚ÄìR‚Ä≤.
  • Amides also have a carbonyl, but attached directly to nitrogen.

‚ÄúOnly single-bonded oxygen‚Äù vs ‚Äúincludes double-bonded oxygen‚Äù

  • Only single-bonded O: alcohols, ethers.
  • At least one double bond to O: aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters, amides.

Example recognitions

  • Ethanol (in beverages and fuels) is an alcohol: CH‚ÇÉ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìOH.
  • Acetone (nail polish remover) is a ketone: (CH‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇC=O.
  • Ethanoic (acetic) acid is a carboxylic acid: CH‚ÇÉCOOH.
  • Ethyl ethanoate is an ester with a fruity smell.
  • Peptide bonds in proteins are amide bonds linking amino acids.

8.3 Reactions of Organic Molecules

Combustion, Substitution, Addition, Elimination & Polymers

üå± Roots ‚Äî Big reaction families in organic chemistry click to expand

Organic molecules undergo several common types of reactions. For this course, focus on:

  • Combustion ‚Äì burning in oxygen.
  • Substitution ‚Äì one group replaces another on a saturated carbon or benzene ring.
  • Addition ‚Äì atoms add across a multiple bond (C=C or C‚â°C).
  • Elimination ‚Äì atoms are removed, creating a multiple bond.
  • Esterification ‚Äì acid + alcohol ‚Üí ester + water (a specific elimination).
  • Polymerization ‚Äì many small molecules (monomers) join to form giant molecules (polymers).

These reaction types explain how fuels burn, how plastics are made, and how many biological molecules form and break down.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Combustion (complete vs incomplete) click to expand

Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon:

hydrocarbon + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + energy

Example: octane (one component of gasoline)

2 C‚ÇàH‚ÇÅ‚Çà + 25 O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 16 CO‚ÇÇ + 18 H‚ÇÇO + energy

This requires plenty of oxygen and gives a clean flame. It is a type of oxidation and is exothermic (releases heat).

Incomplete combustion occurs when oxygen is limited.

Products include:

  • Carbon monoxide (CO) ‚Äì poisonous, colorless, odorless gas.
  • Solid carbon (soot) ‚Äì black smoke or deposits.
  • Water.

Example with methane:

  • Complete: CH‚ÇÑ + 2 O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + 2 H‚ÇÇO + energy
  • Incomplete: CH‚ÇÑ + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí C (s) + 2 H‚ÇÇO + energy or 2 CH‚ÇÑ + 3 O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 2 CO + 4 H‚ÇÇO + energy

Carbon monoxide is especially dangerous in enclosed spaces, hence the use of CO detectors.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Substitution, addition, and elimination reactions click to expand

Substitution reactions

In a substitution reaction, one atom or group on an organic molecule is replaced by another.

  • Typical of alkanes and aromatic compounds.
  • Overall number of atoms attached to the carbon skeleton stays the same.

Example: methane + chlorine in UV light

CH‚ÇÑ + Cl‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CH‚ÇÉCl + HCl

One hydrogen on methane is replaced by chlorine, forming chloromethane. The reaction can continue to give dichloromethane, trichloromethane, etc.

Addition reactions

Addition reactions happen at multiple bonds. Atoms are added across a C=C or C‚â°C bond, reducing the number of bonds between carbons (e.g. double ‚Üí single).

  • Unsaturated ‚Üí more saturated.
  • Typical of alkenes and alkynes.

General pattern:

C=C + X‚ÄìY ‚Üí ‚ÄìC‚ÄìC‚Äì with X on one carbon and Y on the other

Special names:

  • Halogenation ‚Äì add X‚ÇÇ (e.g. Br‚ÇÇ, Cl‚ÇÇ).
  • Hydration ‚Äì add water across C=C to make an alcohol.
  • Hydrogenation ‚Äì add H‚ÇÇ across the double or triple bond.

Example (hydrogenation): ethene + hydrogen ‚Üí ethane

C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÑ + H‚ÇÇ ‚Üí C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÜ

Elimination reactions

Elimination reactions are essentially the opposite of addition: a small molecule such as water (H‚ÇÇO) or hydrogen halide (HX) is removed, and a C=C double bond is formed.

General pattern:

‚ÄìC‚ÄìC‚Äì (with suitable groups) ‚Üí C=C + small molecule

If water is the small molecule removed, the reaction is called a dehydration or condensation reaction.

Key observation: in an addition reaction, each involved carbon ends up bonded to more atoms; in an elimination reaction, each involved carbon ends up bonded to fewer atoms.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Esterification: acid + alcohol ‚Üí ester + water click to expand

Esterification is a specific type of condensation/elimination reaction where a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol.

General pattern:

carboxylic acid + alcohol ‚Üí ester + H‚ÇÇO

Mechanism-wise, the ‚ÄìOH from the carboxyl group and an H from the alcohol combine to form water, and the remaining fragments join through an ester linkage (‚ÄìC(=O)‚ÄìO‚Äì).

Naming esters

  • Take the name of the alcohol, replace -ol with -yl.
  • Take the name of the carboxylic acid, replace -oic acid with -oate.
  • Put them together: alcohol part first, then acid part.

Example: ethanol + butanoic acid ‚Üí ethyl butanoate + water

C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÖOH + C‚ÇÉH‚ÇáCOOH ‚Üí C‚ÇÉH‚ÇáCOO‚ÄìC‚ÇÇH‚ÇÖ + H‚ÇÇO

Ethyl butanoate has the smell of pineapples and is used as a flavoring agent.

üå≥ Trunk ‚Äî Addition & condensation polymerization click to expand

A polymer is a giant molecule made by joining many smaller molecules called monomers. Natural examples include proteins, starch, and DNA; synthetic examples include polyethylene, PVC, and nylon.

When the polymer is made from just one type of monomer, it is a homopolymer. When more than one type of monomer is used, it is a copolymer.

Addition polymerization

In addition polymerization, monomers with double bonds (like alkenes) react so those double bonds open up and link together, with no small molecules lost.

Example: polyethene (polyethylene) from ethene:

n CH‚ÇÇ=CH‚ÇÇ ‚Üí ‚Äì(CH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ)‚Äìn

The repeating unit is ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚ÄìCH‚ÇÇ‚Äì, and n is a very large number. The same idea applies to PVC, polystyrene, Teflon, and others.

HDPE vs LDPE

  • HDPE (high-density polyethylene) ‚Äì mostly linear chains with few branches; chains pack closely ‚Üí stronger, stiffer plastic (e.g. milk jugs, pipes).
  • LDPE (low-density polyethylene) ‚Äì many side branches; chains cannot pack tightly ‚Üí softer, more flexible (e.g. plastic wrap, garbage bags).

Condensation polymerization

In condensation polymerization, monomers react and each time they link, a small molecule (such as water) is eliminated.

Examples:

  • Nylon-66 ‚Äì formed from a diamine and a dicarboxylic acid, eliminating water at each new bond.
  • Proteins ‚Äì formed when amino acids join by peptide (amide) bonds, releasing water each time.

In a protein chain, each peptide bond connects the carboxyl group of one amino acid to the amino group of the next, forming a long polypeptide.

  • Combustion always involves O‚ÇÇ as a reactant and produces CO‚ÇÇ and H‚ÇÇO (plus possibly CO and C in incomplete cases).
