3.1 Relative Atomic Mass

Comparing atoms using mass ratios

üå± ROOTS ‚Äî Core Ideas & Definitions ‚ñº
  • Relative mass compares how heavy one item is relative to another by weighing the same number of items.
  • Law of Constant Composition: Any pure sample of a compound has the same mass ratio of its elements. This implies fixed atom ratios.
  • Dalton used these fixed mass ratios to infer relative atomic masses for different elements.
  • Historically hydrogen was assigned an atomic mass of 1 u and other elements were compared to it.
üå≥ TRUNK ‚Äî Rules, Formulas, and Patterns ‚ñº

For samples with the same number of atoms:

mass ratio (A : B) = atomic mass ratio (A : B)

  • For a 1:1 compound like MgO or SrO, the mass ratio of Mg:O (or Sr:O) is the same as the atomic mass ratio of Mg:O (or Sr:O).
  • For non-1:1 formulas (e.g., BaCl‚ÇÇ, MgCl‚ÇÇ), you must adjust for the number of atoms in the formula when comparing atomic masses.

Example adjustment for BaCl‚ÇÇ:

1.934 g Ba : 1.000 g Cl in BaCl‚ÇÇ
but BaCl‚ÇÇ has 2 Cl atoms ‚Üí compare 1 Ba to 2 Cl

üçÉ LEAVES ‚Äî Examples, Tips, and Interpretations ‚ñº
  • Example (1:1): 0.350 g Mg reacts with 0.230 g O in MgO.
    Mass ratio Mg:O = 0.350 / 0.230 ‚âà 1.52
    ‚Üí one Mg atom is 1.52 times as massive as one O atom.
  • Example (non-1:1): BaCl‚ÇÇ mass ratio Ba:Cl = 1.934:1.000.
    Because BaCl‚ÇÇ has 2 Cl atoms, first divide the Cl mass by 2, then compare to Ba to obtain the atomic mass of Ba.
  • Key idea: you never need the actual mass of a single atom, only reliable ratios.

3.3 Wheel Model of Mole Conversions

The mole as the central hub

üå± ROOTS ‚Äî Core Concepts ‚ñº
  • The mole is the central ‚Äúhub‚Äù that connects mass, number of particles, and (for gases) volume.
  • Direct conversion between mass and particles is not allowed; you must go through moles.
  • Stoichiometry uses mole ratios from chemical formulas to relate amounts of different substances.

Typical conversion paths:

üå± ROOTS ‚Äî Core Ideas & Definitions ‚ñº

3.5 Composition Analysis

Percentage composition, empirical and molecular formulas

üå± ROOTS ‚Äî Core Ideas & Definitions ‚ñº
  • Percentage composition: the percentage by mass of each element in a compound.
  • Empirical formula: the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound (e.g., C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ ‚Üí CH‚ÇÇO).
  • Molecular formula: the actual numbers of each type of atom in one molecule; it is an integer multiple of the empirical formula.
  • Structural formula: shows how atoms are connected, not just their ratio.
üå≥ TRUNK ‚Äî Formulas and Procedures ‚ñº

Percentage composition (from formula):

%Element = (mass of that element in 1 mol compound √∑ molar mass of compound) √ó 100%

Steps to find an empirical formula from % composition:

  1. Assume 100 g of the compound ‚Üí % becomes grams.
  2. Convert each element‚Äôs mass to moles.
  3. Divide all mole amounts by the smallest mole value.
  4. If necessary, multiply all subscripts by a whole number to remove fractions (e.g., √ó2, √ó3).

Molecular formula:

molecular formula = empirical formula √ó (molar mass √∑ empirical formula mass)

  • Tip: If you get subscripts like 1.33 or 1.5, multiply all by 3 or 2 respectively to obtain whole numbers.

3.6 Molarity & Ions in Solution

Solutions, concentration, and counting ions

üå± ROOTS ‚Äî Core Ideas & Definitions ‚ñº
  • A solution is a homogeneous mixture of a solute dissolved in a solvent (often water).
  • Molarity (M) is the molar concentration of a solution: M = mol solute / L solution.
  • A standard solution has a carefully known molar concentration.
  • Ionic compounds dissociate into ions when dissolved; the formula tells the ratio of ions produced.
üå≥ TRUNK ‚Äî Molarity & Ion Relationships ‚ñº

Molarity formulas:

n = M √ó V
V = n √∑ M
mass = M √ó V √ó molar mass

Preparing a standard solution (conceptual steps):

  1. Calculate moles of solute from the desired molarity and volume.
  2. Convert moles to mass using molar mass.
  3. Weigh that mass of solute, dissolve in some water, and then dilute up to the desired final volume.
